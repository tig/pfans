It's a little more complicated than that.  Yes the only way to know for sure
is with an EGA, but it needs to be used with a dyno which is the only way you
can load the engine.  Either chassis or engine will work, but chassis is more
convenient.  Most people who have a chassis dyno use it in conjunction with
an EGR to give you mixture under load which is what you *really* need to
know.  

As far as damage is concerned, too rich will drop the horsepower a bit but
will cause no serious damage unless it's *way* too rich in which case it
could start washing down the cylinder walls.  Too lean is another story.
 This will lead to detonation which can put little (and sometimes big) holes
in pistons and heads. :-(

One final possibility is the use of a portable EGA which runs on 6 (12?)
volts and a helper.  Then testing can be done under load as it needs to be.
 My advice would be to rent a couple of hours of dyno time and sort it out
properly.

If I had to make a guess as to what needed to be done, based on the increase
in displacement and the accompanying increase in CR, it would need to be a
little richer than stock, however there are other variables such as exhaust
system, cam etc. which enter into the equation.
