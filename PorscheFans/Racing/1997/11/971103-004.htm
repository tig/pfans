> 
> Scott Winders wrote:
> > 
> > Dear ,
> > 
> > Just how long have you been running BFG R1's and how good are you at
> > feeling the differences that a 1 or 2 PSI change makes? I know that when I
> > want to decrease understeer, adding a 1 or 2 psi to the rear makes a
> > noticable difference. If you add air to the rear tires on your car and you
> > feel more understeer, you are fooling yourself....R1's just don't work
> > that way! Adding air to an R1 decreases grip.....
> 
> Scott has a point here. R1's have a kindof weird behavior in that tire
> pressure changes to effect grip don't work like other R tires. However,
> the problem comes up in that too many people take that to mean they can
> run extreem pressures to get what they want. No, you can't bleed the
> tires down to next to neothing and get ideal grip. The R1 sidewall is so
> stiff that it can support the weight of the car, but will fold over
> under heavy loading. What happened alot when the R1s came out was that
> people, in the belief that lower pressures were better, were running
> them _really_ low and were then frustrated with the rapid demise of the
> tire (EGOD). One of the BFG guys explained some of this to me. The R1 at
> very low pressure may very will _feel_ stickier, what you're feeling is
> the contact patch rolling over to the sidewall, and tehn the sudden
> break in grip is when too little of the tread is left on the ground and
> the sidewall can't hold you anymore. Where the tire flexed was where
> this "groove" happened to show up. The R1 at 'normal' pressures will
> behave much more predicatble and much less like the "grip and slip" tire
> it was called when it first came out.
> 
> Set up BFGs with tire temps. That is really the best telling way for
> these tires. If you don't have a tire pyrometer, then use your hand. Or
> your friends hand if the tires are REALLY hot. ;)
> 
> -Josh2

> Having worked closely with Yokohama and now being sponsored by Hoosier I have
> a lot of tire expertise. However, I indeed am not a user of BFG R-1's and yet
> I do have  Team T/A written performance data and tire/suspension guidelines
> and it too suggests the same basic "rule of thumb" as I relayed in my

> 
> If I am incorrect then so be it - this forum is good for debate and
> discussion of this sort making it possible for us all to become the wiser - I
> can certainly admit to being wrong.
> 
> It is interesting to read and hear all of the different points of view
> regarding the R-1's. I don't know of any tire with as diverse an amount of
> recommended setup variations. More pressure vs. less pressure, a little
> negative camber vs.lots of negative camber - it's all over the map. The tire
> pressures Mr. Winders noted are on the lower side compared to those that my
> compadres here in SoCal are running therefore I assumed (oh yes I do assume
> on occasion) it would be highly unlikely that adding pressure at these
> pressures would reduce traction.  But then as you noted, and I am fully aware
> of the tire construction, it has a lot to do with how much negative camber is
> dialed into the suspension since the pressure does affect the tire's built in
> camber affect. I actuallly should have asked the original concerned party the
> specifics of his suspension and its settings before making any
> recommendations at all.  I have read a lot of BFG factory reports that too
> many users are running their pressures too low and I have noticed that the
> camber settings are getting more negative dialed in and the pressures are
> coming up with them.

> Subject: 72T vs.72S
> 
> Thad wrote:
> 
> 
> I have a 72 911T with which I would like to have the engine modified to 
> "S"
> specs and begin PCA racing in 1999. (I have much more to learn in 98, 
> more
> drivers ed's, etc.) My question is regarding the class my car will fall
> into. The standard 72 9llT is in the "I" class and the 72 911S is in the
> "F" class. Will the modifications to a 2.4 liter engine move my car into
> GT-4, which I don't want, or if the engine modifications stricktly 
> follow
> the formate of the original 72 2.4 S engine will it be in "F" class?
> 
> Thanks,
> Thad, ARPCA
> 
> Thad, if you only change the engine to the specs of a 72 S, (and if a 
> previous owner hasn't added a few things that could take it out of the 
> production classes) it will be classified as a Production car.   
> 
> Consider staying with the engine as it now is and running in I for a 
> season or so.  You'll likely have more than enough other things to do to 
> get a competitive car (suspension, driving technique, exhaust, 
> engine cooling, etc) to eat up your time and budget.  I  would 
> suggest that you not change the engine until you have to rebuild it, or 
> start winning in I class.  I ran my 73T in I class for awhile until the 
> engine failed and I had to rebuild.  That's normally a large class, and 
> I bet you'll be surprised at how competitive those pesky 914 2.0's and 
> 944's can be, especially on short twisty tracks.  
> 
> Another thing to consider is that F class is normally dominated by early 
> 944T's and 3.2 Carrera's.  I don't recall seeing any 2.4S club 
> racers (I'm sure there are some), and wonder how competitive a 2.4S 
> would be. You might want to run a season or two before committing the 
> dollars to upgrading to 2.4S specs, during which you can solicit the 
> expert opinions of others as to whether a 2.4S is likely to be a good F 
> car.
> 
> If you've already decided, there is a forum member who bought a GT4S 
> with a 2.4 engine, who is planning to upgrade to a 2.7 or 2.8.  Maybe 
> he'd sell you his entire engine and rebuild yours, or perhaps there is 
> some other area for mutual interest.  If you post a notice, he'll likely 
> respond.  I don't recall his name right now.  
> 
> Just my 2 cents worth.
> 
> Lee Wilkins
