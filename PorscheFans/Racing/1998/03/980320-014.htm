Michael Serpe wrote:
> 
> My point is though, is that now the car is set up well, and all done
> through understanding what "BAD" is.
> And it is my feeling that if you never get to feel that and you jump
> into track events in a new c4 with R1's, that by the time you really
> lose it, you've lost it beyond recovery.

This is a very, very important point that Michael brings up. Take the
time to learn the car when _it's_ limits are slightly closer to your
own. And by that I mean, that the limits of the car are lower and therby
a little closer to your (the novice driver) own.
 
> I saw, and not kidding, six 993s get totalled this summer at only 7
> driver's eds on the east coast.
> It seems that these folks are coming into track events with these hot
> performers and never learn to get the car in and out of bad situations
> because the new cars, like an older car with R1's is too good and too
> unforegiving at the speeds that these folks being driving at.

Not unforgiving, but things happen much quicker at 100mph than at 40mph.
That's why when someone asks me about getting into competition oriented
driving that I recomend they start autocrossing first. Now I know a few
well skilled track-only drivers that have very low opinions of autoxing,
I for one think that they don't know @#$%. Autoxing allows you to learn
the limits of the car and how the car will behave at, and beyond, those
limits. And you can do this in a setting where if you loose it, all that
will probably happen is knocking down a few cones. Yes, on a track
things happen much quicker. But the symptoms of 'loosing it' will still
be similar. And with a little experience, you (the driver) will be able
to recognise those limits before you exceed them.
 
> that's not where the problem lies, it's when these guys go to four
> events and feel real confident becuase they're blowing the doors off a
> 912 driven by a 20 year veteran, and equate it to their driving
> technique instead of car.  Next thing you know, they've reached the
> speed where the car actually may slide, and that's so fast that they
> can't handle it when it comes.  because, when the first time you spin
> is at over 100mph, you're in big trouble, as seen by all those who
> witnessed any of the totalled 993s last driver's ed season in the east.

Yup. Last driver's ed I attended (that was a while ago) the only two
cars to hit the armco were an RX-7tt, and a 355 (very sad). The Z-cars,
rabbits, mushtangs and others may have spun, and may have gotten a
little out of shape, but they did so at slower speeds, and had more time
to react and recover.

> My advice:  Start with the shitiest car, tires, and set-up.  It makes
> it more fun at a safe slow learning speed.  You will slide and spin,
> and learn from all of that.  Once mastering it, perhaps then consider
> sticky tires or suspension upgrades.

Well, I wouldn't go quite so far. I'm not going to recomend to a new 993
owner that they go out and buy an ITC fiesta to learn in (although I
think that'd be a _great_ way to do so), I will recomend that they stay
on street tires for a while.
