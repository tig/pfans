Eric Schulhof wrote:

>
>
> 1) Now I don't consider myself a "wimp", but....this car is much less
> "driveable" on a daily basis than my '67 912 was...
>
> I realize the 911T was the detuned, carbureted entry level 911, but it is
> not a fan of stop and go, idling, starting quickly in cold weather
> (California cold, that is) or shot distance commutes. The 912 handled all
> that well...

If you think the T is bad, try driving  an E or S in stop-and-go traffic. These
cars really work poorly below  30 mph. These  cars like to be up in the revs,
otherwise they are a pain.

>
>
> It also has the "sport" transmission with the dog leg first gear so I spend
> much too much time thinking about what gear I am in.

This is not a big deal. However during in-town driving, getting into  first
constantly is tedious. The shifts between 5,4,3 and 2 on twisty roads more than
makes up for first being inconvenient. After some practice, knowing what gear
you're in should be second nature. It is possible your shift linkage needs
rejuvenation or at least careful adjustment and alignment.

>
>
> I find it to be much more of a single purpose sports car than the 912
> which had more (please pardon me) VW/356-ish driveability.

I agree completely. I do not think early 911s (before 1974) are useful for
stop-and-go driving.

> 3) The steering is extremely light...It has wider than original Dunlop tires
> and zero play in the steering, but the steering is "power steering" light.
>
> I understand that this pointed to ball joints in very early 911's but that
> was resolved by 1970. What can this be?

One should have the steering/ball joints checked carefully on a 36 year old car.
However the steering is very light. One of the contemporary road tests praised this
feature saying something like "the steering feels the  same whether one is going 10
mph or 100 mph".  You may find it useful to have the car corner weighed to see of
the suspension is properly set up. It could be you do not have enough weight on the
front wheels.

>
>
> 4) It gets an optimistic 15 mpg in a mix of fast street and freeway driving.
> The "seller" (vague to protect the innocent) proclaimed it would get 20+.
> Is this due to short distance driving/cold running? In addition to this the tail
> pipe is fairly black. The "seller" is adamant that the Zeniths are in perfect
> adjustment.
>

I believe you should be seeing at least 20 mpg  under highway driving conditions.
15 mpg may be normal for short trips in heavy traffic. Take the car out for a 2 - 4
hour drive at 50-70 mph and carefully check mpg. You might want to use highway
mileage markers to check your odometer's accuracy.
