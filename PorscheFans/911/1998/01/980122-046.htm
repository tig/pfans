Alex,

I rebuilt my 82 SC mirrors a few months ago because of the same problem.
It is fairly easy.  There should be a socket cap screw holding the base
to the door.  Unscrew it and remove the whole mirror assy. from the
door.  There is a nifty hollow bolt and spring combination that provides
the break away affect for the mirror that has gotten loose.  The hollow
bolt goes through the base and screws into the mirror shell (if I
remember correctly). You have to use a large Allen wrench to tighten
this hollow bolt.  

The problem arises when you realize that all the wiring for the power
mirror goes right through the middle of the bolt, which is where you are
supposed to insert the Allen wrench as well.  It does not take long to
understand that the wrench and wires can not occupy the same space at
once.  So, snip snip go the wires and you must pull them up into the
mirror assy.  

At this point I chose to take the entire thing apart by starting with
the mirror glass.  It will pry off if you are gentle with it but be
careful to also remove the heating element wires from the back of the
glass.  Then there should be 3 hex bolts/screws you can unscrew to
remove the mirror drive.  This is a sealed part with the thick rubber
edge attached to it as well.  All you are left with should be a metal
shell of the mirror and the hollow bolt/spring in the base.

Unscrew the bolt completely and clean all the parts.  Be careful not to
loose the plastic washers.  After cleaning apply a little multi-purpose
grease to the threaded end of the hollow bolt and screw it through the
base and into the mirror shell with all the plastic parts and spring in
their correct places.  Make sure it is tight and provides a good amount
of resistance to being rotated.  The object here is not to prevent
rotation but just make it hard so if some idiot smashes into your mirror
it will rotate instead of rip off your door.

Put the wires back through the threaded tube.  DON'T forget to put the
little rubber seal on the base now because if you solder the wires back
together then you will have to cut the seal to get it back on (when my
car was painted by the PO they did this BAD BAD).

Now, you have two options here.  You can permanently reattach the wires
or you can put in a connector in case this problem arises again.  I
personally put in a connector because I will be repainting the car again
(previous job REALLY sucked) and will have to take the mirrors off
again.  You can take a short run to Radio Snak and find some nice 6
pin(I think) connectors.  Don't forget to buy the pin removal tool as
well as the connector will not fit through the hollow bolt if you have
to rebuild your mirror again.

So, reconnect your wires and then bolt the base back to the door while
making sure that the seal is even all the way around.

DONE.

If you now have a vibration in your mirror and can not see anything in
it at speed then you might have front wheels that are out of balance or
have flat spot in them.  My mechanic told me this.


Have fun!

Alexander,

 I had loose mirror problems on my '89 Carrera similar to yours, where the
base 
is tight but the upper mirror assembly is loose.  The way I fixed it was:

1. Gently pull the mirror off its base. It may seem tight at first if the
mirror 
has not been removed for the life of your car.
2. There are 3 or 4 phillips head screws that hold the guts of the electric

mirror assembly to the outer shell of the side mirror.
3. The electric mirror assembly will now be hanging by the wires that
control
the motors of the mirror.
4. You should now see a couple more phillips head screws that were
previously 
covered up by the guts of the electric mirror assembly.  They may seem
tight 
already, but I found that backing them out a couple turns then retightening
them 
solved my loose mirror problem.  NOTE: there is another kind of loose
mirror 
problem that is caused when the nut that holds the hollow tube the wires
feed 
through comes loose. Automotion makes a kit to fix it for ~US50.  Luckily
this 
was not the cause of my problem.
5. Installation is reverse of above.

From: Walter Fricke 
To: 911@porschefans.com
Subject: Dropping Clutch

Jim Cunninghame asks about the proper procedure for dropping the
clutch on his 87.  Vol Spaulding cringes.  So do I.

The received standard wisdom is that dropping (or dumping or that even
more graphic description, sidestepping) the clutch on a 911 leads
promptly to dropping large sums of money on expensive repairs.  

I think it goes like this:  in big US V-8s the rear end is pretty light, so
if you
sidestep the clutch you spin the rear tires, which is enormously
satisfying to the driver and impressive to onlookers, what with all the
noise and smoke from the tires.  Because of the weight distribution, the
tires spin pretty easily.  Because they are spinning, they are not working
very hard at propelling the car forward, and as a result the drive train is
not under all that much strain, and the available torque is being
harmlessly (discounting the tires) wasted until eventually the tires do
hook up at the end of the desired black streaks of your tires' rubber on
the pavement, at which time the car is going fast enough that the system
can handle the torque.

Our cars have all that weight over the rear tires (60% of it, more or
less).
 Absent water or sand on the pavement, the tires don't spin under
acceleration, because we don't have NASCAR power levels allowing us
to light them up almost at will.  In short, they hook up pretty well.  If
you
rev the engine up to what - 3, 4, 5,000 rpm, and sidestep the clutch, you
put a huge load on everything because all that torque (4-5,000 would be
at torque peak for many of our cars) is being almost instantly applied.  So
you stand a good chance of breaking something in your transmission, or
maybe even in your engine.  Turbos have larger CV joints because they
are needed to handle their extra power.  I suppose axles could break, or
bad things (other than wear) could happen to clutch components (look at
what transmitts the torque from the outer housing attached to the
flywheel to the pressure plate some time). 

I think this lore may have started with the 901 style transmission in use
from 64-71, where the first gear hung out there on a shaft with a bearing
only on one side, and breaking the shaft there by overexuberant drag
race starts was not unknown.  Expensive part to break, both for parts
and labor.  Makes replacing a clutch look like peanuts.

Whether this remained true with the 915 and your G-50 transmission,
where first wasn't cantilevered I don't know from personal experience.

Nor do I intend to find out.

I have a suggestion for you, however.  Others on this list have driven
more 911s than I (the lucky dogs) and longer (I've been at it since 1984),
but my experience is long enough, I think.  The clutch disk I put in in
about
1986 or 7 I just measured, and it is close enough to the wear limit that I
am going to replace it, though I could get more out of it.  So my methods,
while perhaps not the best, can't be too bad for a daily driven car which
also sees autocross, driver's ed, and Club Race use.  

Start your 911 like you would any car with a clutch - rev it up a bit, ease
the clutch out and match things with a bit more gas as needed until it is
rolling and fully engaged, then do whatever you want.  Clutch will live,
drivetrain will live, tires will live, etc.  You won't be slow off the line
at
the stop lights either.

Where there is a viable reason to increase your initial launch speed, as
at the start of most autocrosses, nothing particularly bad seems to
happen if you start as you usually would on the street, just with a bit
higher initial rpm and a bit quicker easing out of the clutch.  In essence,
you are slipping the clutch more to get the rpm at full engagement up
higher in the torque range.  This isn't doing the clutch (and flywheel and
pressure plate) surface any particular good, but given the relatively few
times you do this, it doesn't look like it dramatically reduces the life of
these components.

What rpm, you ask?  Well, I think most do it by sound.  Maybe one of the
more thorough autocrossers has paid more attention and can enlighten
us.  But I don't think anyone "dumps" their clutch.

Walt Fricke (thinking of his upgraded to SC car as he writes)

From: Walter Fricke

Subject: A short history of brakes

Terry

A short history of 911 brakes up to the monster caliper era of the C2 and
newer (turbos excepted):

The brakes on our cars from 74 through 89 (end of Carrera 3.2) were
almost identical, front and rear, with one exception: For the Carreras
(84-89) the rotors were made 4mm thicker - from 20mm to 24mm,
because the cars had grown heavier and a greater rotor thermal mass
(they are heavier) was desired.  As a result, the calipers were also
made thicker by adding a spacer between the halves.  So you can use
the same pads for all these years.  However, you can't use Carrera
rotors on your SC (new brake pads wouldn't fit, rotor too thick for
caliper) unless you change over to Carrera calipers also.  Which would
be easy to do, but why?

Actually, the pad sizes go farther back:  the rear pad size (sometimes
called the M caliper size - your SC has M calipers in the rear) goes back
to the first 911 in the rear, and for some years (especially in the less
expensive T models) this size was also used in the front (and appeared
there on the 356Cs, and on some 914s).  The fabled aluminum S caliper
in the front took the same pad as the later cast iron A caliper you have
on your SC, although since the S caliper was a bit wider, proper S pads
are thicker when new than A pads, so they won't quite fit in an A caliper
(with a new, full thickness rotor).  This small extra thickness is also why
they cost a lot more.  Worn S pads fit fine in the A caliper, and new A
pads fit fine in the S caliper, they just approximate a slightly worn pad
in
that application.

I think all these calipers were made by ATE, a German brake firm.  I don't
think there are any Porsche part numbers on these calipers, but maybe
there are.  You don't need these numbers, however.  Nor do you need
the Porsche part numbers for the pads.

You can figure out a lot of this by looking at the catalogs.  Automotion
has a dandy table on rotor thickness over the years, but you can see a
lot of this interchangeability by looking for comparable brake pad part
numbers.  Oddities might include the Carrera pads, which have a wear
sensor connection (and so cost more and are given different numbers
by manufacturers who incorporate this feature, which not all the
aftermarket makers do).

Walt Fricke
