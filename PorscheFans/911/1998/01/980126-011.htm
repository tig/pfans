>It certainly is possible to defeat a camera with flash by spraying on
>something which will reflect light.  However, the radar guns which take
>the photos are too far away from the car for the flash to do any good. 
>Flash will only illuminate a subject up to about 50 feet max (you
>professional photographers can give a more exact distance).  When you
>see flash bulbs go off at a football or baseball game, they are doing
>nothing to enhance the photograph of the field because they don't carry
>far enough.  Most often, people will be disapointed because their
>pictures are too dark.  Same principle for the radar.  Smokey would need
>a spotlight to illuminate the license plate in the dark.  Now in the
>daytime, it is different.  You do not need a flash and so a coating
>which reflects back the flash in the day will do you no good.  In
>essence, I am saying that this stuff is probably not worth it.  Just my
>02 cents worth.

>From: Rennsport Systems

>Subject: Re: Nology wires etc.......
>
>Hi Ed:
>
>   Our input on spark plug wires, plugs and related ignition
>woes..........
>
>  IMHO, save your money regarding the purchase of Nology wires. We have
>had excellent luck with using: Bosch OEM wire sets, Magnecor wire sets,
>and building our own as necessary. There is no advantage to Nology wires
>if the existing wires and plug connectors are in good working order.
>
>  In our experience with 911's, one thing truly causes some random
>misfires and other running difficulties is intermittent spark plug
>connectors. These connectors feature a small resistor inside them for
>RFI supression that very frequently, goes bad. A quick check with a good
>ohmeter will reveal this. You must unscrew the connector from the end of
>the plug wire to accurately measure this value. You should see
>approximately 2K of resistance. If its open, you can buy new
>connectors(expensive!) instead of a whole wire set. This is a common
>malady that everyone should check when strange misfires and seemingly
>lack of full power start to manifest themselves. After you have
>purchased several of these connectors, one will begin to appreciate the
>Magnecor wires. They use their own proprietary connectors with no
>resistor and 6K ohms-per-foot wires.
>
>Replacing these connectors is a regular maintenance item. :^(
>
>Spark plugs: Everyone here has their favorites, one should use what they
>have the best luck with, we use Bosch Silver, and NGK V-series with
>excellent results. While not the cheapest plugs available, they offer
>long life and good-running characteristics over a wide range of
>conditions. We also use the old-style Bosch Platinums but they are
>frightfully expensive. In some applications, they are the only way to
>go.
>

>Hi Chris -
>
>You asked about valve covers and headlights on your 911SC.  I have an '87
>Carrera, and I believe my headlights are H5.  The down side is that these are
>very expensive to replace.  They seem plenty bright.
>
>Re the valve cover gaskets, there are a couple of types you can get.  The
>upper gaskets (intake side) are pretty much oil-free, so not a leak issue.
>The lower set do need to make an oil seal.  I've had good luck with the green
>ones that have a narrow bead of grey rubbery stuff going around the edge.
>I've gotten these at Automotion and also at a local foreign auto shop.  After
>8 months of service, the grey bead sticks pretty well, and is damaged when the
>covers are removed.  But after only a few days of service I have been able to
>remove a set with the bead intact, and reuse them successfully.  I install
>them dry, and clean off the mating
>surfaces before installation using a rag with a little carb cleaner.
>
>One important issue with valve covers is to avoid over tightening the nuts.
>The torque spec for mine is 8 Nm, which is INCREDIBLY LOOSE!!!  Get a torque
>wrench for at least the first time, and you'll be suprised.  If you over
>tighten, you can easily bend the valve covers, making them leak prone.
>
>Hope this helps,
>

>To: 911@porschefans.com
>From: troy@videoactv.com (Troy)
>Subject: High Idle for 930 fixed!
>
>** Hello and thanks for all of the assistance with the high idle problem
>with my '79 930.  It turned out the problem was actually two-fold and I
>wanted to share the solution as there have been several people that had
>similar problems with their 930.
>
>A quick recap:  My car would normally idle at 1200-1500rpm even when the
>engine was warmed up and driven for an extended period.  After the engine
>got warm and was shut off, the idle jumped to 2500-3000 rpm upon restart.
>With my dual 4" rolled custom exhaust pipes the noise was very noticeable.
>
>The solution:  We found that the old throttle body had a small hole in it
>which screwed with the air/fuel mix.  So we replaced that and then we found
>that the auxiliary air valve needed to be replaced (thanks Dave Cooley for
>that tip) which cured the high idle when the engine was warm problem.  Now
>the car is at a nice 800 rpm idle.
>
