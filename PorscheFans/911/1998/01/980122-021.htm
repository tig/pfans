According to the physics, it takes a certain force to slide a block of
rigid material across a surface at a constant velocity.  Turn this block
onto a side of larger area, and it will take the same force.  This is
because under the first case (small area), there is greater pressure on
the sliding surface and more microscopic "contact welds" per unit area
than there are under the second case (larger area).  So, the effective
contact welds are the same under each scenario and thus independent of
the area of the block face.  This is only the case assuming that the
material properties are constant and for a limited range of material
properties.

Now, I'll take a stab at the question.  The material properties of a
rubber tire are much different and friction forces developed are much
greater than the theoretical block. Possibly what's going on is this:
imagine that the grip developed at the rubber/pavement interface is very
large, so much so that it can not slide when the lateral load is
applied.  Instead, the first few microscopic layers of rubber are being
stretched/contorted until at sliding, rubber is sheared from the tire.
So, the microscopic shear is not occuring at the pavement/rubber
interface as with the block, but between microscopic layers of rubber
which would be area dependent. 

In addition, as the tire resists the lateral load it is heating up and
becoming stickier, but that's not related to the question because
heating causes the material properties (it's "stickiness") to change.

Just my .02
