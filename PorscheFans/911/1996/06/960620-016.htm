> 2) I have some knowledge since my company makes devices with very 
> complex software embedded in micros, PROMS, EEPROMS, OTP's etc.
> 
> 3) Manufacturers (like us) who spend tens of millions in R and D to 
> develop software which resides in non volatile memory chips are 
> generally smart enough to take steps to protect ourselves from fraud.

I also have some experience in this sort of thing. In a previous life,
I used to write real time S/W for microcontrollers that got burned 
into prom.

Our software was never copy protected. If someone wanted a copy of
a PROM, hell they could call me and I'd send them one. The PROM was
useless without the box to stick it in. Then again, that was a 
different market, and I couldn't imagine anyone making copies.

> I could go on and on and I admit that I have never taken an EFI 
> system apart. I have a chip. I paid for it. It works, I'm happy. 
> Cheap horsepower and I have no desire to make a "backup". Besides, if 
> the guys who write this stuff get ripped off they will either 
> increase the price or stop doing it altogether.

Well, I HAVE taken a system apart. It was from my 86 944. I bought
an aftermarket chip and I wanted to make sure it was programmed
differently than the stock one. I didn't want to get into "The
Empirer's New Clothes" situation. So I loaded the new and old PROMS
into the PROM programmer and compared them. Sure enough, they were
different. And I think that MAYBE I could tell a slight difference
in performance, but not too much.

Anyway, I made copies of both chips. I was surprised at how small
the chips were (16K). I couldn't find any 16K PROMS, but I did find
a 32K unit that looked the same from 0-16K, so I used that. It
worked perfectly. When I sold the car I gave the new owner the original
chip. I think I still have the copies somewhere.

> I have the feeling that modern Engine Management systems probably 
> have protection built in. If I was designing them, I would.

The new OBD systems are very complicated, and definately have something
like this built in. It gets worse. The OBD systems are SO complicated,
and SO comprehensive that it might mean the end of the High Perf
Chip. Then again, the aftermarket can be very clever when it has to.
