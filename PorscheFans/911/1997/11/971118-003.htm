>Before you go pulling out the old oil return tubes try re-seating the seals.
>Mine started to leak, but now they don't -10,000 miles later. Heres what I did:
>
>>From under the car carefully clean oil return tubes and espesially where
>they join head and crankcase, then to re-activate/ re-seat the seals gently
>rotate oiltube and at the same time move axially each direction as far as
>you can.
>My car (an 88 euro coupe) had done approx 50,000 miles then, I think this
>was the 1st time the oil tubes had been touched. The Thermoplastic
>rubber/Viton seals where still quite soft and elastic. Now done 60+ and oil
>return tubes are as dry as a bone.

>Neil - 
>
>You say you hear noise from rear of car, and can move one drive axle a
>half inch side to side, while other won't do this.
>
>I am dubious about being able to differentiate which CV is past its wear
>limit short of taking them all apart (although in your case I would start
>with the axle which you can't move in and out - the axle should move
>without too much force unless maybe Cvs are brand new).  My rule of
>thumb, based on only one instance of noise from each source, you
>should know, is that rear noises in our cars are mainly caused by Cvs or
>by rear axle bearings.  The bearing (assuming only one is bad) makes
>noises when turning the opposite direction at reasonably high loading
>(under acceleration), or at least did so in my case.  The CV makes noise
>when turning either direction.  At least, I think that is how things start out.
> 
>You may be able to tell if a rear bearing is going by feel:  jack up wheel
>and see if there is any play.  There should be no play at all in a 911 rear
>wheel, I think.  If the big nut is tight, it may be bearing replacement time. 
>
>Also, if one CV is bad, rest may be also.  That has been my experience
>where they were all original, or all replaced at one time (as long as the
>boots were all OK).  However, I replaced one axle bearing 8 or 9 years
>ago, other one still fine.  Reaching back into memory, I think the CV
>noises in my VW bus were more like klunks, while I wouldn't describe the
>sound from the 911's rear axle bearing that way.
>
>You probably do not want even to think about that other possible source
>of noise - the transmission.  But if the other sources are eliminated, you
>may have to.   Plus if what you have is a random klunk, sure, lots of
>things can cause that.  Bad swaybar mounts, for instance. 
>
>So, bite the bullet and join the club of those who have spent a truely
>enjoyable and rewarding weekend removing, cleaning, inspecting,
>reassembling, repacking, and reinstalling CV joints.  Even if nothing is
>wrong with them they probably could do with fresh grease.
>
>You can find some good descriptions of CV removal/cleaning on the
>Internet.  Try Nordstern's PCA site tech topics for doing this on a 944,
>copying something from Northern New Jersey?  Same as on a 911.
>
>Walt Fricke

>From: Walter Fricke

>Subject: Torsion bars wearing out
>
>Ron Podhajsky asks if torsion bars wear out.  Scott Winders says that,
>just like springs, they do.  
>
>Ron, who sounds suspiciously like someone who took Materials 101 in
>engine school, promptly posits a mechanism (microcrystaline fractures
>reducing effective diameter).  I am curious as to whether a torsion bar's
>wearing out is related to a change in RATE (which would suggest
>junking them unless you wanted a softer bar?  Or junking them becasue
>they would soon change even more?) or to a change in something else,
>maybe "set."  One hears of older cars sagging (VW vans used to haul
>loads, for instance).  The sources I read usually just talk about resetting
>or using adjustable spring plates to restore ride height, but they are not
>discussing exacting performance requirements.  One also is adjured by
>the manufacturer to release the load on the spring inside one's click-stop
>torque wrench when it is not in use to keep its calibration.  Torsion bars
>obviously have cyclic loads as well as static loads.  Torque wrenches
>don't, at least not when they are not being used.  So is it possible for the
>bar to twist slowly, especially under the static load which is always
>there unless the car is up on blocks, without losing much of its rate? 
>Some sort of slow deformation or creep?
>
>All the authorities speak of is avoiding anything which could be a stress
>riser (a nick in the surface, or corrosion) which would lead to a fracture
>(rate equals zero).
>  
>To know if a bar had lost rate one would think that you would have to
>measure its rate.  Perhaps that is not so easy to do on car, though, given
>bushing compliance, small changes measured at low forces available
>scales might be good for, etc.  Racers who use coil springs can
>purchase testers (not cheap, either) to measure the coil spring's rate. 
>But free height is really easy to measure.  does that suggest a change in
>free height is not a suitable proxy for a change in rate?  (or a distrust of
>manufacturers?)   If a coil spring looses rate when its free height
>lessens (sag), would one not suppose same was case for a torsion bar
>(using the postulate that a spring is a spring is a spring?)
>
>Or is loss of rate sort of like plastic deformation in a bolt?  Once it has
>happened it is a downward trend - if in ten million cycles a 400
>inch/pound bar became the equivalent of a 375, it might only take one
>million for it to become the equivalent of a 350? 
>
>Just curious.
>
>Walt Fricke
