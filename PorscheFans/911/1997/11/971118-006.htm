On Tues, 18 Nov 1997, Scott Winders wrote:

>Let me clarify my "torsion bars do wear out" statement. Torsion bars do
>break and they do sag. A broken torsion bar is warn out! One that sags
>will still work right, but the ride height is altered. You can fix this by
>adjusting the ride height. The spring rate of the bar does not change
>unless there are fractures. A torsion bar with fractures is worn out.

It would seem that if the sag is uniform in all the torsion bars that ride
height is lowered, a very desirable result judging from all who actively
change suspension geometry to achieve this. I have difficulty attributing
sagging or "setting" to simple deformation of the crystal lattice without
the formation of microcrystaline fractures. (Sounds like Materials 101
again, eh Walt) Of course, a major fracture occurs with a series of
microcrystaline fractures. This is facilitated by focused stress in the case
of surface irregularities or singularities. Hence:
