Before you take off the caliper, open a bleed screw and depress the =
brake pedal half-way or so.  Use a piece of wood between the brake pedal =
and the front seat to hold the pedal part way down.  This will help most =
of your brake fluid stay in the reservoir.  Otherwise when you remove =
the caliper, it will all drain out and let air into the system.

I just used a protractor and made one out of cardboard.  You only need =
it as a guide.  The notch is aligned so the rotation of the rotor is =
towards it. If your calipers are mounted on the front of you brake disks =
like on my SC, that means the notch always faces upward and towards the =
rear of the car. The Haynes manual shows this.

I used isopropyl alcohol to clean everything up.  Make sure there are no =

I think Ed misspoke here.  The seal is actually in the bore of the =
caliper.  Take it out carefully (I used a dental pick) and clean out the =
groove the seal was in.  Then carefully fold in the new seal.

When putting the pistons back in, you need to apply pressure with the =
C-clamp to the middle of the piston to keep them from canting.  The =
piston should slide in nice and smoothly.  If not, it's probably crooked =
and you need to pull it out and start again.  Because the piston is =
hollow towards the pad, you'll need some kind of flat plate to put =
across it so the C-clamp has something to press on.  When the piston is =
part way in, check the 20 degree angle and if necessary turn the piston =
to get it right.  You can use pliers, but only on the outermost lip of =
the piston, where the boot goes.

After I got each caliper back together, I polished them up a bit with a =
brass brush on my electric drill.  Be sure to seal the brake line port =
to keep debris from getting inside.  Do this outside, as the old caked =
on brake dust will have asbestos in it.
