I don't recall anyone doing a study of this, but from what I can recall,
the 911 temp gauge that went with the "s" instrument package in the
early 70s was the same one as in my '75.  At least that's the one I
know, so I'll start there--it goes from 120F to 300F, with intermediate
markings at 180 (about 1/4 way up), 210 (about 2/5 way up), and 250
(about 2/3 way up).  That was the beginning of the hot-running cars with
thermal reactors added to the magnesium cases, and to allay fears
Porsche changed calibration in '76 to go to a max of 330!  The other
stupid idea in '76 was the five-blade fan.

What I frankly don't know is whether the unmarked gauge reflects the
300- or 330-degree calibration.  I suspect the former, so you can use
the  above approximation as your guide--that is, when the needle is out
of the cold zone it's at 180, and when it's 2/3 up but not yet at the
red it's 250.  Or you can order a calibrated gauge from North Hollywood
Speedometer, who'd certainly know which one to send you based on your
year car.

My other car, a '90 Cherokee, also has a calibrated temp gauge, and 210
is the normal operating temp for that car.  With some modifications, the
engine in my Porsche usually runs 180-210 and rarely exceeds 220, so I
consider myself fortunate.  The originally-equipped engine
self-destructed in '86 at 66K.  Rarely you'll see a '75-'77 for sale
with very low original miles, but bear in mind the above figure as the
expected life unless you do something that's not quite cricket.

For California listers, I take it SB 42 is still alive, although I'll be
an old man by the time it does me any good.  I keep my fingers crossed
meanwhile.....
