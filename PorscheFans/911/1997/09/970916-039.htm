PDQCARRERA@aol.com  wrote:

> It seems to me that a lot of folks don't realize that just bolting
> on the latest "trick new" stuff (muffler, headers, spark plugs,
> wires,  inlet valve, air filter, fuel pressure regulator, etc.,
> etc...)  is not going to do you much, if any, good if the system as
> a whole isn't tuned to take full advantage of that particular
> component change. We need to be more focused on a system approach
> rather than a components approach.  For examples:
> 
> Decreasing back pressure through the exhaust system can actually
> hurt the torque and/or horsepower if you don't optimize the system
> via timing changes.
> 
> Increasing inlet air mass flow via lower restriction filter element,
> larger throttle valve, boosting, etc... must be accompanied with
> timing and fuel changes to optimize the net effect.
> 
> Spark plugs and various "spark altering" devices alone won't do good
> unless you tune to optimize their particulate characteristics. 
> 
> Any AMENS out there or am I just blowin' smoke (and unburnt fuel)?

Ross:

I'll give you a qualified "amen"... Your point's totally valid, but
only if the engine you're modifying is ALREADY optimized for its
existing components.  If it's not, replacing a component may correct
a problem caused by the FACTORY'S focus on a "components" rather
than a "systems" approach.

In general, the opportunities for bolt-on improvement are in areas
where the factory made compromises in favor of criteria other than
maximum power... Criteria like noise level, ride harshness,
emissions controls, insensitivity to poor-quality gasoline, etc.
