> << My biggest concern is brakes. >>
> Good concern to have indeed! I usually say your brakes should be as big as
> your pocketbook but this isn't necessarily true. Taking into consideration
> the weight of your car and the velocity achievable (read Bhp) the other major
> factor is the type of tracks you race on. Here in California we have a couple
> of tracks that tax your brakes to the max  -- for example at Laguna Seca I go
> through one set of Cool-Carbon brake pads in two days of racing (we get about
> 180 minutes of total track time) while at Phoenix Int'l and Willow Springs I
> get a full 3 events on a set. That's a 3:1 ratio. But  I don't suffer from
> any brake fade at Laguna in a 25 minute Cup Race and have a 2650 lb 3.2
> Carrera with stock calipers, drilled rotors, CC pads and Motul fluid and
> don't loose ground to anybody in my or similar classes (my car is the proud
> track record holder in a tough class).
> 
> << You were one of the only responses who thought S brakes would be OK. >>
> Well just look at the pad size on "S" brakes - they are quite significant.
> Alot of fast light cars run them because of their low weight and relatively
> large pad size. I would probably put them on the rear as well though with a
> brake bias valve.
> 
> << Most people insisted that "big brakes" were necessary. >>
> They are very costly and totally unnecessary for low Bhp applications. In
> your case you would have ~300% more brakes than your tires could possibly
> match in decelleration performance.
> 
> << There is a Twin Turbo brake system for sale including big red calipers,
> cross drilled rotors and more for $2,299.  I imagine I would need to update
> the master cylander.  anything else >>
> Not everyone has the best machined adapters for this application and yes your
> entire brake system will have to be upgraded. That's a very good price and
> accordingly suspect...
>
