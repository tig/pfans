Hi Forrest,

For 'real answers' I suggest you read "How to Make Your Car Handle" by
Fred Puhn, "Tune to Win" by Carroll Smith, and "Race Car Vehicle
Dynamics" by Milliken, et al. They increase in complexity in that order.

The short answers are these:

Reason one: A tire is not a block of wood. Doh! Because it is made of a
material that will distort, the grip is equal to the contact patch. (The
cF stays exactly the same as you mentioned.) For a practical
demonstration, take an eraser and stand it on it's end and move it
across the table top. Now put it on it's face and try the same thing. If
you don't have an eraser, try it with a tire. 

Reason two: You are dead on with your reasoning, but the shape is all
important! With a wider tire you have a wider contact patch transversely
and narrower longitudinally. Hence, it can grip better when subjected to
lateral Gs. Most vehicles cannot overpower the longitudinal grip of the
tires so that is typically not an issue unless you are a top fuel drag
racer. (You can experiment with your eraser / tire on this one, too! ;-)
) When you take dynamic weight transfer into account, the tires with the
most weight can have a _huge_ contact patch in relation to the
unweighted tires. "Bob Bondurant On High Performance Driving" has a good
chapter about "contact patch management" replete with pictures.
Unfortunately, most of the rest of the book is nothing but an ad for his
driving school including pictures of him with celebrities...
