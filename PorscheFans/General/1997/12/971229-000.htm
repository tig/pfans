PORSCHE CAN'T MEET RUNAWAY DEMAND
Porsche just closed the books on a record 1996/97 business year, and is unable
to meet runaway demand for its Boxster and 911 models. The carmaker said it
faced severe production bottlenecks which would cap the pace of continued
profit and sales growth in the current year ending in July, Reuter reports.
Orders, the company said, far exceeded expectations. Porsche said that group
pre-tax profit tripled in 1996/97 to $93.4 million.

Although the company expects sales and profit to grow at least 10 percent
again in the current year, Porsche is still unable to build enough of its two
models to meet demand. Porsche made 32,390 cars last year, and expects to make
about 38,000 this year at its plant outside Stuttgart and through a
contracted-manufacturer, Valmet in Finland.

Porsche's production problems lie with its suppliers which account for 80
percent of production. That limitation makes it hard, but not impossible for
Porsche to expand production at Valmet beyond the 5,000 Boxsters that it
expects to have built in Finland this business year.

Production bottlenecks, however, are a far cry from problems Porsche faced in
the early 1990s when it posted huge losses and appeared on the brink of going
out of business. Porsche has slashed costs, reduced its model range to only
two cars and become one of the auto industry's biggest success stories.

The company said sales in the first four months of the 1997/98 year were up
13.2 percent, with production up 16.3 percent at 9,700 units.

The latest sign of financial health at the company is robust demand for its
new 911 model, a water-cooled, somewhat larger version of the previous 911
which had been Porsche's flagship for more than three decades. The company
said the new car, which is more comfortable and quieter than its legendary
namesake, will appeal to a wider range of customers, helping Porsche break
into new markets.

As Porsche fights to keep production up to demand, it is considering adding a
third car to its product line -- a sports utility vehicle which it hopes will
reduce its dependence on the often fickle sportscar market. Porsche expects to
make a decision on the project by Spring 1998. The company is talking with two
other carmakers about jointly producing the car.
