Hi All,
In following the posts on batteries and alternators I noted one small bit of
alternator information that no one has yet mentioned.

Alternator rotors retain a *small* amount of residual magnetism. That residual
rotor magnetism is supposed to provide enough magnetic field to generate some
field voltage when the rotor rotates. The induced field voltage then further
exites the rotor to produce higher field voltage, etc. until the field voltage
reaches the level where the voltage regulator limits the exitation level to
the rotor thus limiting the output voltage.

Therefore, if you could rotate the alternator connected to either a dead
battery, or no battery (not recommended), the alternator should develop
voltage. Sometimes not, if there is not enough residual rotor magnetism, such
as if the alternator has not been used for a long time. Also, a severely dead
battery could load the alternator field so that it will not generate enough
voltage to exite the rotor to start the charging process. This will usually
not happen when the dead battery is jumped since the good battery will provide
the exitation voltage.

Yeah, I know, an exited rotor must be a sight to behold!
