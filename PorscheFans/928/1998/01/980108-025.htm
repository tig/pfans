On Thu, 08 Jan 1998 "Yu, Vince" wrote:

> But in contrast to a modern EFI system, the current draw is way too
> much for the alternator to sustain without the assistance of a
> battery.  The electronics just simply takes too much power.

If the draw were way more than the alternator could produce, the
battery would be discharging.

> Let us assume for a moment that a '89 GT runs with the battery
> disconnected.  If this is the case, then that tells me that the
> alternator is operating at least 100% efficient;

> Now, what about the EFI system that is needed to manage the
> engine's operation?  This system is a load.  So, if the GT
> continues to operate in this manner, it tells me that the
> alternator is 100%+ efficient.

> NO!  It defies all law of physic!

Actually, it doesn't.  (Having never succeeded in getting away with
a violation, I am very mindful in my obedience to these laws.)

The main source of power into the alternator is mechanical,
i.e. the belt.  Can an alternator generate more power than is
consumed by its field coil?  Absolutely.  Otherwise, it wouldn't
be a very useful device for producing power.

Still, unless the voltage regulator is up to the more difficult
task of regulating the rectified AC output of an alternator in the
absence of a battery, I wouldn't try this on a car with electronics.
