I was reading Barry's post on plug gaps et al and noticed he was 
expecting some comments. Not one to disappoint I'll indulge myself in a 
pet interest of mine. The mechanism involved in generating what would 
seem to be something as simple as a spark could, in fact does, fill 
books. It take a bunch of volts to fire the plug (5 to 20+ thousand 
depending on a whole lot of things) but as soon as the spark is initiated 
and current starts flowing (a spark has a low impedance) and the voltage 
drops to about a thousand volts or so (again the actual value depends on 
lots of things). This thousand or so volts is what is referred to as the 
burn voltage or spark line and is what actually ignites the fuel. So we 
end up with a high voltage for a very short time and a lower burn voltage 
for much longer, of course longer is just a few milli seconds. Opening 
the plug gap will certainly increase the spark voltage and potentially 
over stress the coil insulation if it goes above the design limit so I 
definitely agree with Barry on that one. Especially if you go hog wild 
and double the gap or something crazy like some after market CDI 
manufacturers suggest.  A few thou extra should be fine I would think. 
I'm not so sure I agree about the additional current flow of a smaller 
gap damaging the plug wires though. The amount of current flowing is 
small enough that even a substantial increase would not be a problem. So
