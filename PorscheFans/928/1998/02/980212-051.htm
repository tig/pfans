OK, I now have a quite plausible answer from Loctite, specifically Robert
A. Valitsky, the Manager of Technical Communications for their North
American Engineering Center (call 1-800-LOCTITE for more info), who was
familiar with your call and the e-mail from Jim and me, and called just now
to tell me the following:

Loctite's removable products are affected by what Loctite calls the
"engagement ratio" of the threaded parts. This is the ratio of the length
along the parts in which Loctite winds up to the diameter of the thread.
Their specs assume an engagement ratio of 0.8, reasonable for a nut
threaded over a stud.

In the 928 oil pan scenario, where the bolts are long and skinny, the
engagement ratio may be five times this. A high engagement ratio leads to a
high torque for breaking loose the film of Loctite. For ratios this high,
the breakaway torque may exceed what can be provided by hand tools, or is
safe for the bolt. In such a case, Loctite generally recommends heating the
bolt to about 450 F, but this is inadvisable with an aluminum block.

Loctite's bottom line is that it's not a good idea to use their product in
this application, not because of any chemical reaction between steel,
aluminum, and Loctite, but because the Loctite may be impossible to break
loose without damage.

So, check the bolts whenever you're in their neighborhood, and consider
safety wiring if you're really worried.
