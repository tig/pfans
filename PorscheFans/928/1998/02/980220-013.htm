> Good question - I'm sure we're all going to be following this thread. 
> There are a number of camber plates / tools available by mail order from
> racing catalogs.  Most use bubble floats like spirit levels.  Toe is
> another story but also readily available.  I don't know how easy a home
> alignment will prove without a lift, service dig out, or those 'slidey'
> tire pads (let's the wheels float around so you can adjust settings). 
> This might be one of those jobs where it's easier (and maybe more
> accurate?) to pay a few bucks to the shop you trust?  

See my www page @
http://pantheon.yale.edu/~kgibble/bmw/Porsche_Home_Alignment.html -
wrote it last year.  Certainly you don't need a lift or pit.  Mine's
really low and it's still easy even without driving it up onto 2x4's. 
Mark DaVia and David Lloyd can tell you a little about paying a shop to
do alignments.  We checked 4 928's one day and the 3 out of 4 done by
independents were WAY, I really mean WAY, out of spec.  No doubt some
independents do it right and many dealers blow it.  However, I know I do
it right - or at least I know my limitations.  In my www procedure, you
get some confidence intervals for the camber so you have a pretty good
idea of your errors.
