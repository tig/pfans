> Is there a difference between a Bosch or a Porsche O2 sensor?  I think not.  I have yet to
> change the O2 sensor on my '83 S. (over 200K miles with the original O2 sensor..)  So I
> cannot say for sure.  But I can tell you that on my wife's Volvo, it does use a Bosch and
> it cost $246 for an exact replacement at PepBoys.  I can imagine how much the Volvo dealer
> want for it.  I just decided to cross-ref it with another vehicle that uses the same Bosch
> part number for less than half the cost.  I snipped the connector and splice it into the
> original harness.  All is well.
> 
> Before I did that, I experimented a little... I have a brand new sensor from a '86 Ford
> Ranger pickup.  Just out of curiosity, I did some testing between it and the Bosch.  They
> are both a 3-wire sensor and the thread size is the same.  Both gives out the same range
> of voltages; 0.1 to 1.0.  The differences is that the response time on the Ford sensor is
> much slower compared to the Bosch.  About 3 times slower.  And the heater is about twice
> the resistance, therefore the operating temperature will be different.  I used a propane
> torch to do the testing.  You don't want to heat it up too much or you'll vaporize the
> zirconium substrate.  I was all worked up at this point so I decided to install the Ford
> sensor onto the Volvo.  It ran like crap.  The idle rpm flunctuated between 1k to 1.5k
> every 10 seconds.  I concluded that this is due to the slower response time on the Ford
> sensor.  Satisfied with my experiment, I replaced the sensor again with a new Bosch.  It
> ran just fine.  Not exactly an experiment that follows all the correct scientific
> protocols but one that satisfies me enough to conclude that not all O2 sensors are created
> equally.
