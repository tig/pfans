It is my opinion that lubing the threads AND taper
creates a more accurate torque during the assembly
process.

When the lug starts to bottom out, the sticktion
between the two aluminum faces creates a notchy, uneven
feel to the torquing process.

When reading repair manuals - in order to torque
cylinder heads, the torque specs are usually stated to
apply towards lightly lubricated fittings.  This is in
order to obtain accurate torques.  Otherwise, I think,
the nuts probably won't be tight enough as the
sticktion will stop them from getting that extra bit
tighter.

Also, I don't think galvanitic corrosion should enter
into the equation if the wheels are removed once in a
while as they are for routine maintenance.

On Wed, 11 Mar 1998, GallantCSC wrote:

>Just a thought on proper torque techniques...
>
>Someone (sorry, forgot who) mentioned that the stretch on a bolt changes
>substantially for a given torque, depending on whether the threads are dry or
>lubricated.
>
>Precisely because of this, it is important that the torque is measured with
>the threads prepared as per the manufacturer's requirements.  If you lubricate
>threads that are specified to be dry during the torque process, you'll end up
>with way too much stress on the bolts/studs.  Don't assume that all threads
>should be lubricated.  When assembling engines, most of the torque readings
>are with clean, dry threads, with lubricant specifically prohibited.
>
>Any way, just make sure that the threads are dry or lubricated, as required.
