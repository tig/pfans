I wonder if what you experience is actually the throttle hanging open.  I
just worked on a 79 with that problem.  There are several ball-joints in the
accelerator linkage, a cable, and the throttle body itself, all of which can
wear, dry out, or whatever and develop friction.  Despite only 75000 miles,
your car is fourteen years old and will still suffer old age problems.  You
might start by going through the above items for freedom of movement.

Subject: Subject: 928 hatchback lock and odometer failures

>> Does anyone know the best way to open the hatchback on a 928 when the key
turns but 
the lock does not unlock. I think the linkage between the lock barrel and the
latch 
must have broken. <<

Have you tried pressing down on the hatch whilst turning the key?  I was
advised by a 
locksmith to always do this to keep from having the latch fail and the hatch
fly open.  
The release of pressure may be enough to allow the latch to turn.  BTW, I
have seen 
one other 928 with the lock barrel removed - obviously because of a problem -
so this 
is not an isolated incident.  I haven't tried this, but maybe if you lower
the rear 
seats, crawl back towards the rear, loosen and lower the tool cover, you
might be able 
to at least take a look at the latch.  Since later 928s had electric remote
latch 
release, there may be something there you can push or pull on the release the
hatch 
latch.

>> The speedo works but the odometer does not. Any advice? <<

There is no mechanical cable to speedo, a sender is used, and only electrical

connections exist - at least on my '82.  Must be a problem within the
instrument 
cluster unit.  I'd bet there is a mechanical linkage inside from speedo to
odometer.  
It will have to be repaired.  Removing the cluster is very tedious and time
consuming.  
Locate a speedometer shop who has fixed this problem successfully BEFORE you
start 
dowm the arduous road of instrument cluster removal.  Standard labor time is
around 
3.5 hours.

Subject: Re: Starting Problem

 Phil Tong asked for clarification:
 
>>  What you're saying is the check valve failed so the system had no
"leftover" 
pressure in it for the first fireup right?  <<
 
Well, there is some controversy as to just how long the fuel system does or
should 
stay pressurized after the engine has been shut down.  The pressure should
hold for a 
couple of hours, but surely won't hold overnight.  I believe the check valve
has two 
funtions.  Hold pressure allowing for an easy restart.  Make initial starting
faster 
by aiding the fuel pump in pressurizing the fuel system on initial starts.
 Remember 
that early 928s needed two fuel pumps - one in the gas tank and a helper.  My
guess is 
that a better pump outside the gas tank and one-way check valve was the
improvement.

In any case, anyone who has hard starting should check the usual suspects,
fuel, air, 
and spark.  I'm told that failing (weak) fuel pump or clogged filter often
show up in 
poor running or sudden death.  My car always has run great once started.  Now
it also 
starts great.

Subject: German pronounciations
The comments from Peter Albrecht on the above subject are are correct.
First of all, Porsche is a two syllable word, period. That's the founder's
name,
that's how it's pronounced in Germany. As a single syllable word, it doesn't
sound quite right, does it: What do you do with the "e" on the end?

928: again, Peter is correct. "Neunachtundzwanzig" is the everyday
expression. I
have yet to hear someone in Germany refer to their 928 as a "neun zwei acht".

944: Neunvierundvierzig. Again, "neun vier vier" is a no-go.

Within Clubs or within Porsche AG, people usually refer to the models by
their
simple last two numbers, dropping the 9, hence, a Boxster is a
"Sechsundachtziger", a 911 is an "Elfer", a 944 a "Vierundvierziger", the 993
is
a "Dreiundneunziger", etc. 
Regards,
Serge Dubuc
