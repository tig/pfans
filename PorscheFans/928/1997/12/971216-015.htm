>I understand the need for having a constant pressure on the injector
>to control fuel volume delivery to each cylinder.  However, I think
>having just a fuel damper should be up to the job of keeping the
>right pressure in the fuel rail.
>
>My understanding of the system is that the pump is single speed so 
>it pumps full capacity at all time.  The fuel regulator only let
>so much fuel into the rail, in effect working against the pump in
>some condition.  

I don't think the regulator works 'against' the pump.  I believe that the 
regulator bleeds off excess fuel in order to maintain pressure-- so, in 
essence, it works "with" the pump.

> The fuel damper is there to ensure that the excess 
>gas in the fuel rail gets to go back in the fuel tank.
>
>If this is right, the system would be much simpler if you only
>had a fuel damper.  The pump would keep the rail full and when you
>are not using the pump full capacity (well we do encounter stop
>sign and red lights ;-)  then the damper would open and return the
>fuel to the tank.

I have removed the fuel-dampener on one of my 928s, leaving only a single 
regulator.  The only effect I saw was an increase in audible noise.  So, 
my theory is that the dampeners do two things: 1) smooth out 
*high-frequency* (fuel-pump sourced) pulsations, and 2), provide 
additional regulation for the injectors. There may be other more subtle 
purposes of the dampener, but this is all I have observed.
