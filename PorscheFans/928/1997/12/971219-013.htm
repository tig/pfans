Not even remotely true.  The worst that happens is the judge raises the fine
to the statutory maximum.  The bail listed on the ticket is what you will be
allowed to pay if you do so without a trial (if that is even an option on that
particular ticket, which most simple speeding tickets are).  

A judge has full discretion to fine you to the maximum allowed by statute for
the offense you have been convicted of in court.  

I have had my fine raised twice.  Once just last june.  From $99 to $150.  And
I flew from Virginia to Oregon to make the appearance.  However, I knew what I
was getting into, and I knew that I should have paid the ticket when I found
out what judge I was assigned.  But, I had flown all the way there, and I'll
pay $50 to make the cop shake with anger about being made to draw pictures,
and not knowing where speed limit signs are posted, and not knowing exactly
what the speed limit was where I was pulled over, and not knowing how far we
traveled from when his lights were on, and not knowing where we stopped
finally.

It was great fun.  It was also worth the $50 when the judge said to me, "I
wouldn't have that license plate on my car (MR-WRNG)", and then I got to
sweetly respond, "Well, Judge, you couldn't because I already have it."

Heh, heh, heh.

Oh well, the point is:  make an informed decision about what your time is
potentially worth, and what your chances are in winning or getting it lowered.
