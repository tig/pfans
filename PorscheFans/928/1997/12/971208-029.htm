Adam mentioned a couple of things regarding supercharging that I'd like to
comment on.

1)   Immediate throttle response...
First, Paxton engineers are correct in stating that supercharging has no lag
associated with it as compared with turbocharging.  There are two reasons for
this; the first being that the supercharger is always operating at some fixed
percentage of engine speed regardless of throttle position, so it is by
definition always up to speed.  And secondly it is a positive displacement
type air pump as opposed to a centrifugal type like a turbocharger.  For each
revolution of the pump, it displaces a fixed volume of air into the intake
system, therefore the faster the engine turns, the faster the air is pumped
into the intake system...but always at the same ratio, so the air flowing into
the cylinders is always the same percentage greater than it would be if the
engine was normally aspirated.  So at any speed, the engine's effective
compression ratio is higher, and the same...if it is 15:1 at 4000 rpm, it is
also 15:1 at 1000 rpm.  However, this is only theoretically true because air
flow is not instantaneous and the cylinders will still fill better at some
speeds than at others, but the idea of a constant increase in compression
ratio across the board is the important conceptual difference from a
turbocharger.

The turbocharger, on the other hand, provides a pressure source which is a
function of turbocharger speed, which is not directly related to engine rpm,
but rather throttle position and engine rpm.  Boost pressure doesn't really
rise very much until the turbo is spinning near the top of its speed range,
hence power is kind of mushy down low, especially if the engine's compression
ratio is lowered in order to allow a larger boost pressure.  Sometimes
multiple small turbochargers used in tandem or sequentially are utilized in an
attempt to minimize the lags inherent in their operating principle.

2)  No intercooler required because superchargers run cooler...

The use of an intercooler is unrelated to the method of supercharging; it is a
feature which can be used to good advantage with either turbo-supercharging or
crank-driven supercharging.  As higher air compression is used to increase
performance, the intake air temperature climbs, promoting detonation.  The
intercooler reduces intake air temperature allowing higher compression ratios
than would otherwise be possible.

3)  Uses less fuel off boost than the 6L motor....

Within reasonably measurable fuel consumption, I don't see the difference
between a 6L motor and a supercharged motor.  It takes the same horsepower to
go 50 mph with either engine, the only difference being the frictional losses
associated with the longer stroke 6L motor versus the lower efficiency of
using a supercharger to increase the "displacement" of the 5L motor.  I'm sure
that there are many other subtleties which affect fuel consumption, but I
suspect that they are in the noise, so to speak, and you wouldn't be able to
tell the difference between the two cars in a blind fuel consumption test.
