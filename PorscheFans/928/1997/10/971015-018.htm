Hire one.  If you arent' getting any satisfaction by now, you are not likely
to get any.  Do you have copies of the contracts, did you get the driver to
sign off on all of the intransit damage, do you have the inspection sheet
from load time, have they specifically denied any liability, did you pay them
or Lucky, did he pay with check or credit card, was the car otherwise
insured, ETC....

These are some of the questions that go into analyzing the viability of a
claim against the shipper.  It is likely that you haven't spoken with the
right person at that company yet.  You need to get very aggresive RIGHT NOW,
time is your enemy.  

I have had good and bad experiences, all with the same company.  I always
dealt directly with the owner, or his son, and all damages were promptly
fixed.  On the larger problem I got him three estimates, and settled for an
appropriate amount.  

All of these truckers claim policies are governed by the P.U.C., or whoever
licenses them in those parts.  They have strict, timelined, rules that you
must meet in order to have a valid claim.  I would suggest you contact
someone that deals with freight claims often, you will spend less by hiring
someone that is experienced right from the start.  I would imagine that the
company that you are dealing with is self insured, and either administrates
the insurance themselves or pays an insurance company to administer their
claims.  You may be able to solve this with a couple of simple letters and
phone calls.  But START NOW.

I would like to hear from folks on the topic of tire pressures for the 928.
As you should know the recommended tire pressure is on a label on the inside
of the fuel filler cover. On my 86 the recommended pressures are: 36 psi for
the fronts and 44 psi for the rears.

With over seven years of driving a 928, and having replaced four sets of
tires,I can say that those pressures are designed to make sure the car
understeers whenever one drives near the limits of adhesion. Over the years
I have noticed certain tell-tail signs as to what the pressures should be
(at least for me). First, as my tires wear down the fronts show excessive
wear on the edges but still good tread in the center. This usually means
"underinflation". Second, the rears wear out in the center first which
usually means "overinflation".

I now run the pressures at 40 psi all around and find that the tires wear
much more evenly. Also, there is considerably less understeer. However, the
likelyhood of going to oversteer is increased accordingly. I caution
everyone that decides to try my set up about the oversteer aspect.

Good luck and happy motoring. Ed

Subject: Re: on-car tire balancing 

Thought I'd chime in on this thread.

On-car balancing is a good tool to have in your hip pocket for 
situations *real* tire balancing doesn't work.

A *real* off-car dynamic balance job balances the tire/wheel about
3 axis rather than the single axis of your car's axel. A tire that
does not shake at speed may pull and make the car feel out of 
alignment.

If an on-car balance cures problems an off-car balance couldn't, then
you were balancing the wrong thing in the first place. I'd guess your
brake rotors are out of balance.

I had a car on jack stands for a number of years, without wheels
mounted. Developed a nice pile of rust dust under each brake rotor
where the rotors simply fell apart. In a heated garage. Tapping the
rotors caused even more rust to fall out.

As big as the 928's brakes are, it wouldn't take much unbalance to
cause problems. Look inside your front 928 rotors, mine have balance
weights.

above normal at the end of 15 laps.

It's pure magic!

>Now that silly plastic cup for the shifter is another story.  Definately
>a PITA to service since the ball it rides on just happens to be mounted
>on the forward part of the torque tube.  I have no idea why they did
>that instead of allowing for a shifter mounted design to facilitate
>easier servicing.  Ohh.... never mind!  Anyways, the only symptoms I

 Man, I got so sick of dealing with the plastic-lined ball-joint at the 
top of the torque tube that I re-engineered the whole thing.  I also had 
problems breaking the small threaded shaft between the ball-joint cup 
itself and the rod.  Both of these problems were due to very hard 
shifting (going into 3rd), mostly on the track, but several times on the 
street.  Eventually the darn thing will pop out due to wear.
 Anyway, my fix was to take a piece of 1.5-2" diamater/3-4" long Teflon 
square stock and machine a hole longitudinally slightly larger than the 
shift link itself. I also machined a shallow curve on one side to mate up 
against the torque tube.  Then I took the ball-joint off, slid the Teflon 
bushing down, then re-attach the ball-joint.
 Now I needed to secure the Teflon bushing, so I took two very long SS 
hose clamps and clamped the heck out of the bushing to the torque tube 
(clamps go around the torque-tube).  To further stabilize the it, I JB 
Welded a couple of "stops" onto the torque tube, on each side of the 
bushing.  I did not bother putting any lube on it, since it was Teflon.  
Now I can shift as hard as I want with no fear of popping the ball-joint 
out, and my shifts are more precise.

Marc
