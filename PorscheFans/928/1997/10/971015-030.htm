>   My experience has been that no amount of balancing (whether accurate or
>   not) can get rid of the shaking that tends to occur when the compound
>   on a high performance street tire starts to get hard.  On many high perf
>   street tires, the compound is softest at the outside of the tread and
>   tends to be harder toward the inside (i.e. closer to the wear bars).
>   Between the harder compound that is exposed as the tire wears and the
>   natural process of the rubber getting harder as the tire ages, performance
>   degradation is inevitable.  More than once I've had to replace tires that
>   still had a decent amount of tread just because the hardened compound was
>   causing shaking and unacceptable levels of grip.  I've had this problem with
>   Bridgestone RE-71s, Bridgestone Potenzas, Yoko A-008, and Yoko AVSi.  I did
>   not experience the problem with Yoko A-008Rs (a soft compound autocross tire).
> 
>   Supposedly, the Dunlop SP-8000 has a new type of compound which is
>   designed to provide a consistent level of hardness throughout the entire
>   life of the tread.  I put a set of these on my wife's car, so I'll
>   see how my experience matches Dunlop's claims.  I believe that the SP-8000
>   has been superceded by the SP-9000, but I would expect the same compound
>   characteristic.  BTW, after driving on them for a few months, I do not
>   consider the SP-8000 to be a great ultra-high perf tire.  They're fine for
>   the type of driving my wife does and they offer excellent wet weather
>   traction, but they're definitely not 928 material.
