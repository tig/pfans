Dave:

You have symptoms often associated with plugged exhaust.  Before we get too
carried away,  we are assuming that the other mechanical condition of the
engine is OK.  A compression check is a yardstick measurement to make sure
we're not fighting a holed piston or bad valves.

Plugged exhaust is diagnosed with a vacuum gauge attached to the intake
manifold.  With the gauge attached and visible while you drive, try
accelerating gently on level ground while watching the gauge.  If it rises
to atmospheric with no load change while accelerating, look at the catalysts
for blockage.  Under normal circumstances,  a steady foot will have the
manifold pressure drop under rising RPM, thanks to the engine's attempt to
pump more mass at higher RPM.  The steady throttle is the key here.

Plugged cats frequently cause oil leaks, as the crankcase pressure rises and
there's no scavenging of the burnt gasses into the intake system.

Hope this helps.  Cats aren't cheap these days,  but still better than
chasing your tail for the solution.


Good luck, and please let me know how good my amateur diagnosis turns out.
