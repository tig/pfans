INCREASES IN 928 RESALE PRICES

We have been subscribing to "Old Cars Price Guide" for more 
than a year and a comparison over that term shows an nice 
increase for some 928 resale prices, and a slight decrease 
for one model year (1990). 

Here is my critique and analysis of OCPG's 928 prices.

I invite your further critique of the OCPG guide, and your  
critique of my critique. Brenda and I have been to a number 
of classic car auctions and find that the guide is a pretty 
good, well, guide. But it is far from infallible, which the 
publisher doesn't claim to be. Also, we find a fairly good 
comparison with raw auction prices as published by Kruse 
Auction Company in their annual.

Below, first I list the prices for each year, for ready 
comparison among models, as shown in their October 1996 
issue. Then the same models (except 1990) as shown in their 
December 1997 issue, which arrived in our mailbox in mid 
October.

Then I list all the same prices in pairs for ready 
comparison between years.  For each year, I show the percent 
change for condition "2" cars.

Then I compare the price changes for all the years. Also, I 
show a bar graph of the changes.

Finally, I provide the addresses of the publisher of the 
guide, and Kruse. (Note that the publisher is Krause 
Publications. So far as I know, there is no relation with 
the differently spelled Kruse International.)

In both lists, all prices are shown in order of condition 
"6" thru' "1". A "6" is a parts car. A "1" is a time machine 
to a Porsche new-car show room of the respective year, 
except maybe for the odometer in the case of a 100 point 
restoration. Most cars you see at classic car auctions are 
"3s", though perhaps over-described by their sellers.

PERCENT CHANGE "Oct 96" to "Dec 97", AND GRAPH.

To the right of each year, I have made a bar graph with a 
period at the 0 point. I put the periods in a straight 
vertical line. Depending on how your software converts from 
mine, you may have to align the periods to see the graph. 

If aligned, you can note breaks in the otherwise smooth
curve of the graph at the introduction of the S, in 84, and
again at the introduction of the S4, in 87. It appears that
the plain 928 from 78 thru' 83 has its own curve, the 928S 
from 84 thru' 86 has its own, and the S4 from 87 to the end 
of the data has its own. For the plain 928, it appears that 
the later models have increased more than the older ones. 
For the S, it appears that the opposite is true - but there 
may be too little difference to make much of it. For the S4, 
the older ones appear to have gone up more than the newer. 
And, for the 89 S4s, there has been a slight, 8%, 
depreciation.
