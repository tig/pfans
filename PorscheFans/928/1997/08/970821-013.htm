>>The temp II sensor (on my 85 at least) has 2 thermistors in it with one 
>>end of each being connected to ground via the shell of the sensor. One of 
>>the wires goes to the injection computer and the other to the ignition 
>>computer. The thermistors have a higher value when cold which tells the 
>>injection to richen up the mixture and probably (I say probably here as 
>>theory and practise are not always the same thing and I haven't verified 
>>this as yet) tells the ignition to advance the timing a bit as a cold 
>>engine can stand bit more advance and needs it for best efficiency. Your 
>>rogue resistor is effecting one of those two functions. You are getting 
>>more fuel or perhaps more advance than the designers intended. This could 
>>be an attempt to hide another problem such as a vacuum leak or perhaps a 
>>misguided attempt at improving performance. The colour bands you saw 
>>should actually be read the other way around i.e. red black orange gold 
>>which tell us its a 20K ohm 5% device. This is a very high value compared 
>>to the value of the thermistors so one of your computers will think your 
>>engine is very cold all the time. I'm not at home at present so don't 
>>have my reference material but 20K will surely translate to something way 
>>below freezing. If memory serves, the thermistors are normally 200 to 300 
>>ohms with a warmed up engine and around 2 or 3K at 60F or so. I've got to 
>>believe that the resistor is in the ignition circuit as I doubt the 
>>engine would even start when hot with a value like that in the injection 
>>circuit. You would also be measuring fuel consumption as GPM!
>>As you say, this would not be a standard item. I would certainly short it 
>>out and see what happens.
