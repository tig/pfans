Mark Commins wrote...

>I have an '85 928S with 84k on the clock.  I bought it about 3 months ago
>and have driven it only about 1,500 miles as family and job pressures
>keep my free time to almost nil.  A few weeks ago, I was driving the car
>home at about 90mph when the engine shut off as cleanly as if the
>ignition had been turned off.  I coasted to a stop and while I had plenty
>of battery, I could not get the engine to fire, or even cough.  I brought
>the car home on a flatbed and the following weekend I looked at the
>engine.  Good spark, good fuses and relay to fuel pump.  On the chance
>that the gas gauge (reading 1/4 full) was erring, I added one gallon of
>gas and the car started immediately.  Easy repair, I thought.  I took the
>car to a gas station one block away and fueled it.  The amount of gas it
>took indicated that the fuel gauge had indeed read correctly.  I drove
>the car one more block and while sitting at a light, the engine died.  I
>got a couple of sputters out of my starting attempts, but then nothing. 
>I came back an hour later and tried again, no good.  I came back several
>hours later with jumper cables and got an immediate start.  I then drove
>home.  I have started it several times since then, but am afraid to go on
>the road because I don't know the cause of the failure.  I bought a fuel
>filter and hoped to change the original and to find it full of water. 
>However, I have not invested in the manuals and I'll be damned if I can
>find the fuel filter.  Help me please!

>Anyone, have you ever heard of a problem similar to mine?  I am baffled.

Mark, I've had a problem almost identical to yours.  Full throttle on the
San Diego Freeway and ... dead silence as the engine quit dead.  Restart was
successful, but ran into *very* bad performance, sluggish, etc.  Mechanic
couldn't find problem, so tried a tune up.  No go.  Then I heard the fuel
pump making lots of noises.  Silly me, I thought it was the pump causing my
problems.  Nope.  The fuel pressure regulators on the firewall were replaced
by the mechanic and no more problems.  

By the way, the vac. line running to the fuel pressure regulators snapped
somewhere during the process so I don't know if it broke before or during
the repair.

Anyway, he found it by using a fuel pressure meter and monitored the
pressure as the engine was run.  He said the pressure read perfect for a
while and then just shot up off the scale.

Good luck on your problem.
