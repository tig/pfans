Jack Norton wrote:

> Stéphane, have you ever flushed out your brake fluid?  Rarely is this done,
> even though it is recommended every two years.  What happens is that the fluid
> will be dirty and full of moisture over the years.  Brake fluid (except the
> dreaded DOT 5) is very hydroscopic, and readily absorbs moisture from the air.
>  Not only will the moisture screw up the master cylinder, but it will also
> cause sticking problems with your brake calipers.  The contaniments make the
> rubber seals swell which prevents the pistons from reliably retracting.
> 
> You can use the buddy system to pump it out or use a vacuum tool as sold by
> Automotion.  Buy several *Quarts* of fine fluid, but be extremely careful as it
> is an effective paint stripper!  (For a car that hasn't been done in a while, I
> do this twice, with several weeks between fluid exchanges to allow all the
> contaniments to mix with the first fluid flush.)
> 
> If you really want to do a fine job, pull out your brake pads, and allow the
> caliper pistons to come out a half inch or so.  (But be careful - there is
> nothing holding them from coming completely out.)  This way you can clean and
> inspect the pistons for pitting and corrosion.  If you notice any leakage or
> cracked boots, there are caliper rebuild kits available that allow you to
> replace all the seals in the caliper.
> 
> The water in the brake fluid lowers the boiling point of the fluid and corrodes
> the system internals.  So if you run it hard at the track, your brakes will
> stop working when the fluid boils (This is very exciting when it happens...)
> 
> It seems to me that professional mechanics don't like to do this much because
> it's time consuming and kind of a messy hassle.  But if you take good care of
> your calipers, I see no reason they shouldn't last forever.
> 
> If the calipers are working properly, the pads don't drag on the rotor. I hope
> this helps!
>
