At 06:02 PM 7/17/97 -0700, you wrote:
>no petroleum oil is even in the same league as even the worst of the
sythetics

Not that non-synthetics will cause your engine to disintegrate, but good
synthetics protect enough better to justify their higher price. They also
offer noticeably (to me, using Amsoil Series 2000 20W50) smoother running
and lower engine temeperatures.

>Second, I'm nearly positive that the 928 shop manual calls for 10W40,
>not 20W50!

Nope. I just looked in the shop manual, and it shows 20W50 as the
recommended weight for all but the deepest winter temperatures (constantly
below 5 degrees F) for all 928 models from 1980 forward.

>A thicker oil might be called for in a HOTTER climate, certainly not a
>colder climate like the Pac NW.  For instance, on the Rally of the Lost
Patrol
>(Seattle to Inuvikand back), we switched most of the cars to 5W30.

See above. Synthetics are much more tolerant of low temperatures than
non-synthetics. I've never had a problem with congealed oil in the winter
here.

>Check out comparisons by non-biased sources, such as:
>http://alicudi.usc.edu/~jan/vw/FAQs/faq.oil

Thanks for the tip. However, there are lots more parameters of interest and
importance to specifying engine oil than are treated in that article.

>Red Line is THE top-performing oil available, period.

Not so, even though it's widely used by racers. Amsoil publishes the ASTM
four-ball wear test results for its Series 2000 and most other synthetics,
including Red Line, and the Amsoil product held the wear scar down to less
than half the length of the second-best (Mobil 1); Red Line was further
down the list.
