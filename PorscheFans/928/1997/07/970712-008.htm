To the Porsche 928 Crowd,

Someone (possibly a friend) posted a writeup I did on the Lamborghini
Countach to the 928 list a few days ago.  Please note that I posted this to
the Ferrari list and didn't intend this for the 928 or any other car list.
In fact, I no longer subsribe to the 928 list since I sold my two 928's
some time ago.  I don't mind someone sending this along to other car lists,
but it's important to understand were I intended this writeup to be
discussed and debated.

It's a little unfair to bash away at my dreams on your list when 1) I
didn't post to your list, and 2) most of the comments are presented in a
forum in which I can't defend myself.  That said, here's a few added
comments based on some posted responses:

The Lamborghini Countach, like nearly any Ferrari, can't be justified by
any rational argument as a practical or cost-effective automobile.  That
isn't the point.  The Countach is as much a hobby-car and emotional issue
as anything.  I've owned 928's.  They're great cars.  But I sold them for
Ferraris and Lamborghinis.  

>2 months to grow comfortable enough to steer with one hand.>
>
>It took less that 2 minutes to get comfortable in a 928.
>

Exactly the point.  Driving the Countach is still an exotic experience;
driving the NSX and 928 were essentially like driving my daily commuter
cars.  Exotic doesn't mean better or worse, just something different to be
relished and enjoyed.

>< I bought the car through a dealer in Illinois, and I paid $28,000 plus a
>1993
>Acura NSX in trade.>
>
>What a waste of some serious cash.  He could have kept the NSX and spent
>the 28K on a nice 928 GT.

One motivation for my purchases was retention of value.  Let's see who's
cars are selling for what in 10 or 20 years.  My opinion is that the
Lamborghini has reached the bottom of it's market value.  This is supported
by the rising prices on older Countachs and Miuras.  I'll bet you can pick
up a good 928 GT for $10k in 10 years.  Similarly, NSX prices are falling
like rocks.  I figured I better trade my in while it was still worth
something.

>owned.>
>
>But how does it compare to the build quality of Porsche?

Much better in my opinion.  I've owned two 928's (1978, 1982 S) and both
had considerable problems compared to both the Countach and the 1977
Ferrari 308 GTB (70k miles) I have.  The electrical gremlins in the 928's
are well known.  I'll still take a good carbed car over fuel-injection for
the home mechanic.  

>either side of the wheel in order to fit during racing.  Tall drivers need
>not apply.>
>
>It is already well documented how the 928 can accommodate drivers of all
>sizes.
>
Note this comment was with a helmut on.  Without a helmut, the Countach is
fine.  And, I've had similiar (but not as severe) problems with helmuts in
the 928.

>12 cylinders, 6 carbs.  Simple?   Give me 8 with fuel infection.

Yep, simple.  No complicated emissions.  One carb throat per barrel.  With
a working knowledge of Webers and a set of mercury sticks, tuneups are easy.

>hesitation on acceleration.  I talked with another 5000s owner who
>confirmed this problem, and a re-read of several Countach reviews from
>80-85 also mentioned this trait.  I found that the acceleration pumps on
>the carbs weren't injecting near enough gas on throttle opening.  Thank
>goodness for Webers, since a quick alteration of the pump bypass orifice
>resulted in about a three-fold increase in gas delivery on acceleration and
>completely eliminated the hesitation.>
>
>This is simple?
>

Yes, it is.  A similiar description of the workings of the Bosch K- or
L-Jetronic in the early 928s would be considerably more complex and beyond
the capabilities of many DIYers. I worked on and diagnosed a fuel/vacuum
system problem on my older 928, and found this job just at the limit of my
abilities.

>for the first time.  The most amazing fan was a 9-year-old boy >

This is out of bounds.  Liking and admiring this car has nothing to do with
maturity or age.  I said the *most* interesting fan was a nine-year-old.  I
didn't talk about the 70% of people who approach the car and admire it who
are adults.  My second favorite fan was an 80-year-old grandmother.  

>Seriously though, I actually thank Joe for his post.  It simply reinforces
>my opinion that the 928 is the best sports car value on the road today.

Don't thank me, I didn't post this to your list.  Enjoy the 928, it's an
exceptional car.  I'm certain I'll own another one someday, probably a
late-model GT or GTS when prices come down in a few years.  However, I
wouldn't class the 928 as a pure-bred sports car.  It's a GT, and probably
the best one ever built (maybe the Ferrari 456GT is better, but not on a
value basis).
