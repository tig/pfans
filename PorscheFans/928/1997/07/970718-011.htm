JW wrote:
> However, I do understand that cavitation is the formation of partial
> vacuums in a liquid by a swiftly moving solid body or high-frequency
> sound waves.  I do not believe it is cavitation caused by a water
> pump that is the singularity degrading the process of heat
> transfer. It is the 'cavitation' in the rest of the system (where
> the process of heat transfer is in play, as differentiated from the
> process of coolant flow), and specifically in the radiator,
> galleries in the block and heads, and interconnecting pipes and
> hoses that is of concern.  The high-frequency resonance set up by
> the reciprocating mass, flame front, exhaust gas pulses, etc., and
> the swiftly moving liquid through rather narrow spaces of rapidly
> changing dimension (this particular variable causes significant
> speed differential within this closed system), geometry and
> direction (this particular variable causes significant acceleration
> differential), and all of which surfaces are varying in smoothness
> and porosity, that are the problems: Water Wetter seems to provide a
> partial solution. It is the 'lubricating' properties that allow
> Water Wetter to work by reducing the friction between the coolant
> and the hard bits of the system, or so goes the manufactures pitch.

I am certainly far from knowlegeable about fluid flow, and I have no
wish to offend, but this sounds like hogwash. Water is extremely good
at transferring heat, and wets metal surfaces well. The water pump
might turn a little easier with lower surface tension, but I don't see
that having any significant impact. Lets say some cavitation occurs in
the pump--so what? It might decrease the volume of water pumped
*slightly*, and it may take a *small* toll on horsepower, but it seems
unlikely to cause much trouble thermally. I think that there is no way
that there is any other source of cavitation--I don't see the part
about "high frequency resonance".

Sorry to rant on about this, but it just doesn't seem to make
