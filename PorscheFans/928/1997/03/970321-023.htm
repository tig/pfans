>Well, the timing belt tension is critical on all 928's - that belt
>is a monster, and the engine grows a lot as it heats up (there's
>a compensator to cope with that, but nothing's perfect). BUT, on
>a US '79 the pistons and valves won't try to occupy the same
>space at the same time if the belt breaks (if your heads haven't
>been planed) so a belt failure or skip will just make the engine stop
>running. On a Euro '83, timing belt failure will cause very
>expensive interference.
>
>Now, there are surely a lot of mechanics who will just tension the
>belt until it can just be twisted a quarter-turn, and some of
>those will get the tension right, but you really can't be sure
>it's right unless the factory tool is used.
>

I've changed my timing belt twice already and in both instances, 
tension it using the quarter-turn method; then make slight adjustments 
within a few hundred miles.  You are definitely right about the engine 
growling if it is too tight.  Have anyone tried using a tensioning tool 
aside from Porsche's?

Also, a piece of advice, check the rubber cap on the thermostatic 
tensioner.  If it feels like hard plastic, change it soon.  Mine failed 
while I was driving.  Not fun...

>Well, while I am waiting for the parts to come in to change the 
>timming belt, I am looking around the engine bay and trying to figure
>out where all that **** oil came from.
>

When you get a chance, remove the driver-side cam gear housing, the one 
directly behind the cam year along with the distributor.  Behind  it, 
you'll find an oil galley about the size of a dime as well as a small 
neoprene washer.  This washer tends to shrink over time and therefore 
loses it's sealing property.  I bet that's where the majority of your 
oil leak comes from.  Reinstall using a good quality sealant.  
Apparently Porsche does not use any type of sealant on the mating 
surface.  It took me almost two years to figure this one out.
