I can't stop myself from further pomposity, so here goes.

If your brakes can maintain a constant retarding force (and thus a constant
negative acceleration a) over the range of speeds from
whatever-you-were-doing-when-you-noticed (with or without binoculars)
something-interesting-ahead (call that speed V) through zero (stopped), the
time that it takes to make that stop is proportional to V (T = V/a as long
as you use consistent units), and the distance to make that stop is
proportional to the square of V (D = V^2/2a).

Here's a table of stopping times and distances for various initial speeds,
assuming a = 1.0 G (maybe attainable on today's best street ultra tires
with balanced front and rear braking; right, Kim?) and disregarding
reaction times (those of anyone on this list are of course approximately
zero anyway):

speed (mph)	stop time (sec)		stop distance (feet)
40		1.8			54
60		2.7			120
80		3.6			214
100		4.6			335
120		5.5			482
140		6.4			656
