My ex SO is a credit union president, and contributes the following--  The
loss payee on the policy is the credit union.  Whenever the coverage is
changed or cancelled such that the credit union is exposed, the insurance
carrier notifies the credit union.  The policy at this credit union is to
pick up the coverage and bill the member for it.  In addition, you may be
asked to pay off the loan in full if it's determined that you intended to
defraud the credit union.  It is certainly in the insurer's best interests
to continue to carry the coverage, so that interest is best served by
notifying the loss payee.


My opinion:  The savings realized by cancelling the comprehensive/collision
coverage is negligible.  While your risk of being hit is reduced or
eliminated while the car is being serviced,  it is still subject to theft or
vandalism.  Perhaps the exposure in the shop's lot is greater than at home
in the garage, too.  I don't know what your comprehensive portion is, but a
three month cancellation of just that part saves me less than $100.  With
luck on my side as it always is,  something would happen.  It just does...


Like all insurance,  you are gambling that you will crash the car while the
underwriter bets that you won't.  You lose your ante no matter what, and the
carrier can raise the stakes at will, especially if he sees that you are
getting bad cards dealt to you.
