> Greg S., in a recent posting to the digest, indicated that installing
> a 55
> bar brake force regulator "should reduce front end dive and let the
> rear
> brakes do more work."
> 
> Changing the brake pressure proportioning valve will do nothing to
> alter the
> amount of front end dive that is experienced under hard braking.
> Regardless
> of where the braking force is coming from (front only, rear only, or
> some
> combination of both), the amount of front end dive will be the same if
> the
> deceleration of the car is the same.
> 
> Altering the pressure proportioning between the front and rear brakes
> will
> affect the split of total stopping force, but not the amount of front
> end
> dive, if the total stopping force remains the same.  Front end dive is
> related to the deceleration of the car and the spring rate of the
> front
> springs.  If you want to decrease the amount of dive, you need to
> increase
> the spring rate...for example going from a spring rate of 170 lbs/in
> to one
> rated at 250 lbs/in.
> 
> Also, the only reason I can think of to reduce the braking pressure to
> the
> front wheels in relation to the rear wheels is if you routinely lock
> up the
> front during your autoX adventures.  Increasing braking pressure to
> the rear
> wheels may cause them to lock significantly before the fronts, in
> which case
> all you will have succeeded in doing is to reduce the total amount of
>  braking force available during all out stops.  The "ideal" situation
> would
> have all 4 wheels lock at the same time, allowing you to get the most
> out of
> your braking system before going out of control.  Obviously anti-lock
> does
> help rectify this situation, but the principle of getting all 4 wheels
> to
> brake to their maximum without locking is the idea behind selecting an
> appropriate pressure proportioning valve.
> 
> Good luck with your autocrossing.
