It's been about 15 years since I had a driver's ed class but I could have
sworn the instructor taught us that anytime you lock up just the rear
wheels of a vehicle, you can expect to be quickly looking back from whence
you came.  I know it doesn't sound logical, but he used a model car to
demonstrate the effect that locked rear wheels had on a moving vehicle and
it always flipped around bass ackwards.  Perhaps our resident FZICIST can
enlighten us on the physics of this phenomenon.  Leonard?

I can see using the handbrake gently to provide a drag on the car from the
rear wheels, but on a slick surface, you'd need to be very careful to
prevent lockup.  I checked out the brakes of my 928 (sans ABS) in a parking
lot shortly after I bought it and was able to lock the front wheels before
the back.  The car was impossible to steer but showed no inclination to do
a 180.  Of course, the rear brakes were trying hard to stop the car, also.

To All:  Whats all this misinformation about spinning when you apply the
handbrake?  Has anyone had it occur?  .....The emergency/parking/hand
brake works on the rear wheels only and is a valuable tool in safe
driving while in motion. Perhaps some of you have worked out on a
skidpad.  When they do a spin lockup on the brakes its on the front
wheels only which causes an immediate spin. Just think it through. If
the front wheels want to stop and the rears keep going, even power off,
your car will want to swap ends.  If you apply brakes to the rears only
it is like a gteat hand grabbing you from behind and pulling you back.
A spin wouldn't result without some strange inputs from the driver.
  I use the hand brake all the time in winter, particularly on slick
downhill grades where you know sliding will occur. You slide, But it is
very controllable.  Try it some time this winter and you'll gain some
new repect for that lever.
