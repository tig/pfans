Yo, Phil:

No, using the 300hp euro/CIS motor would NOT be cheating.  I'm simply
comparing what is REASONABLY possible with a 305 Chevy... Assuming:
        1) The kind of assembly that both Porsche & competent aftermarket
                build-up implies
        2) Balanced crank, reasonably strong & lightweight rods & pistons
        3) Lightweight valvetrain
        4) Sensitively ported heads
        5) Appropriate carburetion with well-designed intake & exhaust manifolds

All told, this makes a 305 Chevy a roughly 265-300HP (SAE net, as per
Porsche's own HP claims) match for the 288HP of my '86 32valve 4-cam engine.
Will it last as long as a 928 engine?  Depends: 928 belts & inability to
overbore the Nikasil liners vs. the Chevy's maintenance-intensive
valvetrain.  One thing's for sure, the cost to run the Chevy 200,000 miles,
including initial cost & rebuilds, will be a fraction of the 928 engine.
Certainly, the total "$/HP/Mile" will be less.  Want 350/400+ hp?  Both
engines will do it, at substantial costs in longevity & drivability, but I
haven't speculated through this comparison.  As I noted in my previous post,
at these power levels you begin overriding the ability of streetable
suspensions, brakes & tires to actually deliver the goods, so the greater
gains come from "simplifying & adding lightness."

By the Bye, while Ed Ruiz's "ludicrous" doesn't hold IF YOU JUST COMPARE THE
ENGINES, it certainly holds if you consider how much of the 928's special
qualities (balance, traction, systems integration, etc.) would be lost in
even the most sensitive engine swap.
