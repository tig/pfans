> Impressions:
> At the Glen:  It was my first school at a track other than Lime Rock.
> It took me about 2 sessions to be "on line", or close at least.  On
> Sunday, we started working on braking later and not lifting between turn
> 1, through the esses, up to the chicane at the end of the backstretch,
> as well as before turn 10 (left hander preceding front straight).  At
> first, I was entering the esses at ~80 and, at the track out of the
> final right hander, close to 100 mph and then accelerating on the
> straight to ~ 120 before the chicane.  Things went together well on
> Sunday - entering the esses at 100, exiting at ~125, and accelerating to
> just below ~135 before using those wonderful brake pads to avoid
> collecting all those pretty orange cones surrounding the chicane.  There
> was clearly more to be had - I was being conservative (there’s no prize
> money after all) and was not full throttle through the esses (despite my
> instructor’s encouragement) - exiting at 130+ and a straight-away speed
> of 140+ is clearly within the car’s potential.  I wasn’t trying to get
> all the speed I could - this is just what came naturally after becoming
> more & more confident about the line and the speeds speak volumes about
> the car’s hp, stability, and the wonderful R1 tires.  (I was in the
> novice run group so there were some interesting closing speeds coming
> onto the back straight and in the braking zones.)

> Questions:
> The new pads don’t have an accommodation for the lining wear sensors.
> Does anyone have a worn out set of sensors that they could send me so I
> can neatly turn off the brake pad warning light?

The brake pad detector is simply a wire encased in plastic.  When the
plastic get worn off, the wire gets broken and the light goes on.  In
your case I would either leave the detector on and tape it to the brake
line
or other out of the way position or remove the sensor and install a
jumper
in the plug.

> 
> Near overheating:  In the last 2 runs on Sunday, the ambient temperature
> was between 70 and 80 and we were using the engine at pretty high revs.
> The temperature gauge climbed to just below the red zone so I’m afraid
> something needs attention.  One thing I’ve already noticed is that at
> least one fan is always running whenever the A/C button is depressed and
> the key is on (even if the engine is not running).  For someone else
> with an S4, can you check if yours does this?  (I believe I have a bad
> temp sensor somewhere but I doubt this will contribute to the near
> overheating.)

Couple of thing you might want to try:
	Red line water wetter: this additive has had some good review by the
list member...
	Change your coolant mixture:  The glycol we add to our cooling system
is not very good at carrying heat when compared to water.  You could
flush
your system and fill it exclusively with water if you live in an area
where there
is NO chance of freezing.  The saffer way is to go to a 60% water and
40% glycol
which give you protection for temperature below freezing and added
cooling 
capacity.  (This mixture, according to Porsche Tech Spec book, give you
protection
to -25 Celsius (-13 F)).

>
