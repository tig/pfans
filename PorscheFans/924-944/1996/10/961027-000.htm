>The parts which circulate water into the interior of the car
>are separate
>from the stuff you were working on (at least in the earlier
>cars).  Double
>check that there really isn't hot water circulating into the
>interior of
>the car by either touching the metal hose on the passenger's
>side, under
>the center console, where it feeds into the interior radiator,
>or else by
>hitting the "defrost" button with the heat turned up all the
>way.  If you
>still don't get any heat, check in the engine compartment for
>the water
>shutoff valve and see if it's stuck or something (although it
>normally gets
>stuck open, not closed).
>
>If you're for sure not getting heat into the interior, then try
>disconnecting either one of the two vacuum lines to the
>solenoid-operated
>vacuum valve on the passenger's side.  If you remove the glove
>box, it's
>easy to access.  There's two valves, both marked Nippondenso.
>The lower
>one is what controls that shutoff valve in the engine bay.  If you
>disconnect either one of the vacuum lines and you have hot
>water, then the
>valve needs to be replaced.
>
>If you are getting heat into the interior radiator, then you've
>got a
>problem similar to one posted here recently, which is probably
>a small
>plastic piece on the driver's side which controls the flaps
>that direct the
>incoming air past the radiator.  That's what it was in my car.
>If you poke
>your head underneath the driver's side with the engine off and
>the key on
>(so the fans work), you can fiddle with the temperature control
>and you'll
>notice a small rod which moves a flap.  The plastic on the end
>of that rod
>was the culprit on my car.  Hope this helps!
