Excuse me.

  Am I missing something here?
  Even those of us who dont race, find their
brake fluid looking black.  On 944's in particular
the clutch is a hydraulic system that shares the
brake fluid at the reservoir.  From the pumping
action of the clutch during normal usage, the fluid
may be getting some circulation and making the
reservior fluid appear contaminated (which it probably is).
  I'm sure the master cylinder contributes to this as well,
but on the 944 it seems to happen faster than other cars
that dont share the clutch servo with the brake reservoir.

  I perform a careful flush of the reservoir, using an
ear syringe to remove the fluid as far into the reservoir ports
as possible.  I wipe out the container with clean paper
towels (no lint) and refill the reservoir *before* a pressure
flush of the rest of the system.
  Even then, some gink remains in the clutch servo system.
  Unless you go the extra yardage to flush that too. (fanatic!)

  I really doubt most deserving PO's go around with their
brake reservoir uncapped, or their systems open for very
long.    The black crud comes from the wearing rubber
parts in the hydraulic circuits and slow oxidation of the
metals in fluid contact. (imhe).  Racing would seem to
accelerate the process?

  -kyle

>Brake fluid can decompose in two ways. If your fluid is perfect and there 
is
>not a single drop of water in it, the high temps from heavy braking will
>decompose the components. Now, if there is any water, the water can 
catalyze
>chemical reactions and accelerate the breakdown process. Two track days
>(depending upon the track and how agressively you brake and how wet the 
fluid
>is) could do it. The decomposition reactions will continue to occur (albeit
>at
>a much slower rate) when the car is just resting in the garage. It is just
>about impossible to keep your brake fluid dry. The fluid just sucks the 
water
>out of the air. The decomposition reactions are inevitable which is why
>people
>who take their cars to the track consider brake fluid to be a consumable 
item.
