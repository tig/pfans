Hello Ezra & P-Fans
	Yes - Ezra, it is normal for the engine to take a few compression strokes
to reach the compression test max value, usually the gauge will reach 
its max reading in 4 - 5 revolutions. Remember to perform the test with 
a warm engine (oil temp of 80' C Min.), coil wire disconnected, and shorted 
to ground (to prevent coil/ignition ECU damage), throttle plate (WOT) open -
yes that last one will affect your readings! 
The first gauge reading should be about 75% of the Max end reading. All I 
meant to say that if your motor takes 8 compression strokes before you 
get that Max reading - something is worn out on your motor. In four to five
strokes, you should get the Max compression reading. 

	If your readings are low, or they take more than five strokes to reach 
Max, you might want to do a "wet compression test" or a "leak-down test". 

	A wet compression test is when you add a little amount (a table spoon
 full) of oil into the cylinder and then re- do a compression test. The oil
will 
help to temporarily seal any worn rings - boosting the measured results.
Little or no increase in the compression readings would indicate leakage
elsewhere, probably from valves, or a failing head gasket. 

	A leak down test - will use a special tester and a supply of compressed 
air, each cylinder is pressurized. The gauge will show a percentage of the 
air leaking out of that pressurized cylinder. Generally a good reading is from
0-10% leakage, 11%-15% marginal wear, and anything over 15-20% it's time 
to consider a rebuild. Snap-On sells leak down testers from about $160, I've 
seen some for even around $100 for a kit.

Hi P-Fans.  

Well, the combination of a few days off and the newly acquired S2 sitting in
my garage has me up to mischief.  I have been wanting to improve the throttle
tip in on the car, which, as usual with Porsches, is very gradual.  On my
1985.5, I made my own "throttle response cam" by simply cutting off the long
arm on the cam, extending the rounded slot for the cable with a small round
file, and relocating the roll pin that held the cable end.  It worked great
and I saved the $30 that Automotion and others want for the same idea.

On the S2, the throttle is opened via a series of bell cranks which attach to
an "L"  shaped lever arm that attaches to the intake manifold.  The arm has a
ball fitting on each end.  If you add a new ball fitting halfway up the arm on
the side to which the throttle cable attaches, you get a greater amount of
throttle opening action for the same relative amount of cable (and ultimately
pedal) travel.  I bought a new ball fitting that had a threaded end, drilled a
hole in the arm, and attached the fitting with a nylock nut.  With a little
adjustment of the cable, the geometry works out fine, with no binding.
Although this change does nothing to add horsepower, it makes the car feel
much more responsive, just like the throttle response cam does on the '85.

Thought I'd pass this along to the list.  Merry Christmas and Happy New Year
to all.
