>>I switched the original unit with a Weber big-bore bought from a guy on this
>>list (he converted to Weber carbs instead). I think throttle bodies in
>>general are claimed to give a 10% power increase. Don't know about the
>>Weber, but it definitely feels like it. I have an automatic, so any change
>>in power is quite noticeable.

>Some points to remember when trying to extract more
>Hp from a engine. 
>
>A 10% increase in airflow (larger throttle body) 
>doesn’t get you 10% increase in Hp! A 10% increase 
>in air flow (CFM) will result somewhere around 1-2% 
>MORE Hp. Yes increasing the CFM will give you more Hp, 
>BUT if the throttle body can flow more CFM’s that does 
>not mean that the intake manifold, cylinder head,
>valves, exhaust and all can also move that extra air 
>flow!

But do the intake manifold, cylinder head, valves and exhaust all run at max
capacity from the factory? (I don't know anything about the technical stuff
here, I just report what I feel when I press the pedal..)

[...]

>	I would not reccomend anyone use the Weber 
>big-bore because of the all open or closed nature
> of the throttle body. Your engine will feel like 
>a toggle switch. There is no progressive modulation
>of the throttle any more. I think this is why people
>think these mods make more power. They do not!
>	
>	I think alot of people confuse a different 
>torque cuve for an increase in performance when 
>making modifications to their engines. Bolting a 
>larger throttle body or fuel pressure regulator 
>WILL change the way the engine accelerates and 
>the torque cuve - and not always for the better!

Oh yes it was for the better. Even if it didn't increase the overall power
of the engine (I have no way of testing this), it sure increased power at
low RPMs. Like I said, I have an automatic and the increase in power, if
only at the bottom of the scale, is very noticeable and _very_ welcome.

>Alot of times the modification slow the engine 
>from reving up (say with too much fuel from a 
>modified fuel pressure reg) and the driver thinks 
>hey the car has more power, it feels different - 
>wrong, you lost power actually! The only way to 
>tell for sure is to make measurements. Wether
> you make them with a G-Tech, a Vericom, a 
>stopwatch is up to your resources. Do your test 
>in gear - no shifting over a broad RPM range.

I did test it with a stopwatch, and the difference before/after the K&N and
Weber was about 1 second from 0 to 60mph if my memory serves and my
stopwatch didn't flip out on me. Measurements like these are to be taken
with a huge grain of salt, but the car sure wasn't slower nor does it feel
slower. Anyway, like I said - even if the only thing it did was shift the
torque curve it was still a very worthwile improvement which I don't regret.
