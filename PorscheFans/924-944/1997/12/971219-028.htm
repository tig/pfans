In a message dated 97-12-19 12:42:16 EST, you write:

<< Torque on a fastener is a function of the force of the fastener shoulder 
 on the mating surface, not the threads. >>

What do you think reacts against the mating surface force?  The force exerted
on the threads.  If there was no force exerted by the threads, then the bolt
would come right out.  Thats why there are threads on there, and that's why
threads strip when you overtighten stuff, more force is applied to the threads
than the material can physically support.  

I have a BS in ME, but I'm 4 yrs removed and haven't been working in the
field, so I can't give a precise theory based lecture.  As far as I know,
specified torque values are for a clean dry thread.  Theoretically, you could
list two specified torqes, one w/ lubricant, and one w/o.   Here's my attempt
at explaining the theory.

When you tighten a fastener, there are two primary forces involved.  Both of
these forces are originating at the thread mating surfaces.  One force (we'll
call it TF for thread force) is developed by one thread face pushing against
the other thread face when you screw the fastener in/on.  Until the two items
you are bolting together meet, the screw moves inward with little resistance.
Once the items make contact, there is no place for the fastener to go, so it
deflects (or stretches.)  Without getting into stress and strain equations,
the more you tighten the fastener now, the more force is exerted between the
treads.  This force on the threads acts perpendicular to the thread surface,
which is slightly at an angle from the centerline of the fastener.  This force
can be broken up into two other forces, one that acts along the centerline of
the fastener (and is equal to the force that the two items being held together
are subjected to) and a the other is at a tangent to the thread, and causes a
torsional force to be exerted on the fastener.  The second primary force is
the frictional force between the threads which acts in at a tangent to the
threads as well.  This also adds a torsional force on the fastener.  

So now you have the force along the centerline of the fastener (which is the
critical force involved, such as the clamping between the head and block).
This is the force that must be a certain value depending on what you're doing.
However, you can't measure this outright.  The only way we can get close to
measuring this force is indirectly through the torque applied to the fastener.
This force is always proportional to the torsional force applied by the
threads pushing on each other.  The additional torsional force due to friction
is then added to the thread's pushing torsional force, to get the specified
torque.  

When you add lubricant to the fastener, you decrease the frictional force.
Then, when you set the fastener to the specified torque (which is equal to the
sum of the frictional torque and the thread applied torque) you are actually
setting it with a higher thread applied torque (because you have less
frictional torque) and this corresponds to a greater force along the
centerline of the fastener (i.e., your clamping force or pre-load.)

wrote/erased a couple of times trying to explain it.  Again, if we were
sitting down w/ pen and paper, I could explain it better.
