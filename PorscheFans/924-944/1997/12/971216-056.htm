I don't know -- it is difficult to mark the lower front shock mounts so
accurately as to reinstall without changing camber. Porsche recommends a
new alignment after changing any suspension components including shocks.

> Unless the new units change the ride height (likely on high
> pressure mono-tube designs). Raising the ride height will decrease the
> amount of negative camber you have.

I don't understand this. Using a higher pressure shock absorber may
raise ride height, but how will it decrease negative camber? Negative
camber increases whenever vertical forces at the wheels are increased,
within the tolerances of the drop-links, tie-rods etc. A higher pressure
shock will exert (near) vertical pressure in both directions. The
component of force in the upward direction will result in higher ride
height, but the component in the downward direction (every action has an
equal and opposite....) will result in increased negative camber.

Aside from suspension adjustment, the only time negative camber should
decrease will be in the event of significantly lowering vehicle weight,
and/or tired/lo-pressure shocks. If the sum of vertical forces on the
wheels is decreased -- such as when the frame of the car is going up on
a hoist -- negative camber will also decrease rapidly.
