> Don't worry about the rear letting go once.  It could have been anything from sand on
> the road to low pressure in a tire.  If I felt that it was becoming a pattern then the
> first thing I would do is check out the tires for tread depth and wear patterns as well
> as tire pressure.  Could be that the rears just got a bit too worn.  If all looked well
> I would then hike on over to my friendly local wheel alignment center for a 4-wheel
> alignment check.  Finally I'd go autocrossing just to check it out in a safe environment
> and to experiment with tire pressures.  Things change all the time and the challenge is
> to try to stay on top of it all.  Even experts loop one once in awhile!
> 
> Note: The 944 is sensitive to brake shock in a tight corner.  If one pops the brakes in
> a manner that pitches the car nose-down and tail-high while entering a sharp, slow
> corner then the inside rear wheel camber changes & lifts (and a bit of toe-in change too
> I think) as it nears the end of its travel.  That can quickly change the friendly
> natural understeer into a snappy oversteer.  Sometimes an autocrosser might want to
> induce this behavior but it could be a bit scary on the street.  This is a driver
> control item but it is also affected by shock absorbers (worn or fresh).  By gradually
> getting into the brakes while still on the gas before blending out of the gas and harder
> on the brakes, one can settle the car into a level squat and then zip through the corner
> as on rails.
> 
> Answer to your question: Yes, if the front end sticks TOO hard then the rear would be
> the first to let go and that is oversteer.  The trick is to find a neutral balance.  One
> might actually have to decrease front stick to balance the car and thus go faster.  I
> used to have an autocross car in the H Stock where I could leave the front tires at 45
> lbs and base the rear tires at 38 lbs.  A 2-lb higher change in the rears would cause
> the car to understeer while a 2-lb lower change would make it oversteer.  It was GREAT!
>  I could tailor the car to fit the various courses.
