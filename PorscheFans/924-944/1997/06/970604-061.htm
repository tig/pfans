 Farzaan,

          > Paul B's rear shocks are set stiffer and we found that his
          > car is actually easier to drive (transitions faster) and
          > understeers more.  It seems that stiffening up the rear
          > shocks from the factory settings has actually made the
          > back stick better, thus increasing understeer.  This is
          > not entirely unusual, all theories, etc, about
          > suspension all work within a certain range

I'm not sure this is contrary to theory.  From physics,
the whole mass-spring-dampener system will equilibriate fastest
when it's 'critically' damped.  More damping than this and the
response slows down, less damping and the body roll (and weight
transfer) overshoots and rebounds before it equilibriates -- so
the response slows down again.

Does anyone know if shock adjustments typically straddle this
critically damped point (which depends on spring stiffness and
roll inertia -- so "typical" is ambiguous)?

To physics, add the driver  -- maybe the ideal is to have
weight transfer equilibrate on the order of the driver response
(or 'perception') time, rather than as fast as possible.

Comments anyone?  I'd welcome a discussion of this.
