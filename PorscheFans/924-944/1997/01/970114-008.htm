>1.  What is the proper method for starting my 944 on a cold winter
>morning?  Is it good for the car to allow it to warm up for awhile prior to
>driving it or should I just get in, start it up and drive?

I give it about 15 seconds to get oil to the various nooks and crannies
of the engine, then drive it gently until I start to feel heat coming out
of the 
defroster vents. 

>
>2. The reason for question 1 is that I allowed my car to warm up this
>morning and next thing I know, the car starts to sputter and then stalled. 
>When I tried to start the car, it acted as if the battery was completely
>dead and wouldn't even turn over.  If I let the car set for a few minutes, it
>would try to start and then nothing.  I decided to give it a jump start
and it
>worked.  However, if I let the car idle to long, it would sputter again and
>stall.  Finally, after I jump started the car the second time, I had to
get in
>the car right away and take off.  Now everything is O.K.  I've had the car
>for a year now and this only happened to me one other time but then I
>had the timing belt, water pump, balance shaft belt, etc  replaced and it
>never happened again until this morning.  

Very strange behavior. Does sound like a battery with a surface charge that
was
enough to start the car, but when idling did not get a recharge, and
eventually
drained the battery enough to let it die.

Yep, that's plausible enough... A good battery is cheap enough that you should
consider its replacement if it is three years old or older, even as a
diagnostic.

>
>I find it hard to believe that it is my battery because the car started right
>up this morning without a problem.  I would appreciate any insights to
>what the problem may be.
>

Batteries are black magic to me... They can hold a nice surface charge, enough
to start your car, but not have any depth of charge to them. I usually end up
replacing them after three years, just because the aggravation of a jumpstart
on a cold morning is not worth the extra twenty bucks I am saving...
