>
>First off, let me give you a little background.  I am mechanically

>maintained myself, performing everything from oil changes, water pump and

You clearly possess all the talents required to do the job.   It ain't rocket
science.

>
>The car seems to run well.  The engine idles rough, but accelerates well
>and runs smooth.  There is a lot of play in the driveline, and some
>"whine", but it isn't excessively loud.  The shifter needs help, it is VERY
>loose, and I suspect it needs bushings replaced somewhere.
>
>My questions are:
>
>1) Is the car worth ~$3500 I'm going to offer for it?

Not for an 82.   They did not start production until 83.

>
>2) Can I fix this thing myself for a reasonable price?

Depends.   Keep the oil fresh.   Parts are not free, but no worse than
many Japanese cars.   A fifteen year old Porsche will require some care.

>
>3) Can I spend more time driving it than fixing it?

Generally not.   These are NOT early British sports cars.

>
>4) Are the problems I have noticed normal for this car at this age and
>mileage?

The shifter looseness is typical and fixable, cheap.   The rough idle should
not be tolerated and could mean a motor mount failure, frequent in 944s.
Medium
expensive to replace.   I don't buy cars with unidentified whines.

>
>5) What other pit-falls are there I should watch out for?

If they cannot give you a service receipt showing the date the timing belt was
changed, or the date is nearing 30,000 miles with all the required inspections,
AND you still buy the car, TOW it home and change the belt with the required
$500 tensioning tool. 

Listen for deep engine knocks at idle.   If you hear it, walk away with all
your money.  

944s, properly cared for and represented, are WONDERFUL cars to drive.   A lot
of them end up on used car lots with questionable histories for all the wrong 
reasons.   A 944 this old should be considered a parts car until the seller can 
prove otherwise.

Hope this helps.
