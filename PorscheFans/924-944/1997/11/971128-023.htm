This is typically (assuming the rotors were never turned) caused by
heat-soaking (not letting the rotors/brake system cool enough before
stopping the car after a run).

If the shudder is *really* bad, you may have lucked out and all the
heat-soaking has done is deposit a layer of brake pad material on the rotor
at a particular place. This is easily noted by a dark outline of the brake
pad on the rotor. If this is the case, the whole issue will go away after
3-4k km's of driving. This happens more often than you think.

>going to have all the rotors checked and turned to make sure they're the

I personally wouldn't run (typically) turned rotors on the track. Often too
much material is removed, which seriously compromises the heat dissipation
abilities of the rotor.

>problem. Meanwhile, thought it might be time to consider some decent
>slotted rotors, which I understand will last longer than cross-drilled

Most places that do slotting will slot your rotors. That way you can buy
factory rotors of known quality and have them slotted. Be aware the
slotting *will* reduce pad life, and should only be done as a last result.
I investigated the situation fairly thoroughly earlier in the year, and
decided to put a cool-brake kit on my S2 in lieu of slotting rotors.

In answer to your original question :-), KVR Performance
(www.nikon-kvr.com/KVR_BRAKES.html) does slotting (and drilling), but
probably isn't cost-effective for you to use (then again, the CDN$ is so
low that it may be worth it).
