>On the thread of fwd:  Although there have been a few instances of fwd in
>racing (i.e., at Indianapolis back in the late 30's(?) and perhaps a few
>others of note), it is my understanding that the primary design thrust of
>fwd has always been packaging (dating back to the 1959 Mini anyway).  That
>is, stuffing the whole drive train in one little box on the front of the
>car leaves more room inside the car for a given weight/size of car.  While
>it is generally true that the more mass over the driven wheels, the better
>the traction (witness locomotives), when it comes to performance oriented
>cars one must be careful how much is asked of the tires on the driven
>wheels.  In fwd, the front tires are asked to provide tractive force AND
>the lions share of lateral cornering forces.  This is O.K. for mini vans
>and sedately driven machinery.  It would also be O.K. on cars that are just
>posturing as performance machines.  However, in the context of serious road
>racing, tires are generally happier if they have these two functions
>separated.  I know there have been successful 4wd road racing cars (Audi)
>but even here, the front tires are not being asked to provide all of the
>tractive force.  This is not to say that fwd cars cannot be made to handle
>well as I'm sure many people who race showroom stock CRX's and Golfs, etc.
>will attest.  The bottom line though, in my opinion of course, is that no
>matter how you slice it, fwd is a big compromise.  It's really not just
>tradition or coincidence that F1, CART and even NASCAR for that matter
>(ever notice that the cars they try to 'sell on Monday' are fwd, unlike the
>race cars?) are rwd without exception.  I believe you will never see a
>Porsche, BMW or any make that strives to be taken seriously as a
>performance machine with fwd.  As long as performance minded driving
>individuals see it that way, such makes won't be caught trying to foist
>this compromise on their customers.
