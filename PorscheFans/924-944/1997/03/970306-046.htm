> what I need to know:
> 
> 1. how possible is it that I'm in over my head? I want to learn, but, it's
> tough getting the experience... I don't want to count a trashed head as
> "experience" even if the manuals are detailed to the hilt, I assume it's
> still likely to find myself crying in the front yard laying on my back with
> greasy black hands waiting for lightning to claim my soul...
> 
You're not over your head if you are prepared to take your time with
this and ask questions.  If the car can sit until finished is the big
problem.  If you've got other transportation while it takes up garage
space, you're in good shape.  When you aren't sure what to do, ask 
questions.  That's what this list is for!

> 2. I don't really know what I'd be trying to do... my intial strategy is:
> 
> a. replace as many gaskets, replace as many seals around balance shaft
> belts, camshaft covers, and oil cooler as possible...
> 
You can get a front seal kit to take care of most of these.  I've never worked
on a non-turbo, so I don't know what's involved with the oil cooler.
You might as well replace the camshaft cover gasket if it's leaking at
the same time.

> c. replace the radiator (the drain plug is stripped anyway)
> 
Why replace the whole radiator, for a stripped plug?  Surely you can
find another plug!

> 3. those damn belts... are they really as difficult to get right as I hear?
> I'd replace the rollers and stuff while I was doing this, so access should
> be a little easier than some of the stories I've read about...
> 
They aren't that bad.  They just take time.  Replacing everything else won't
help since it all has to be back together before you start on the belts.
Replacing the rollers along with the front oil seals and belts is a logical
thing to do.  Don't even consider starting this until you've got a 
tensioning tool in hand!

> hmm... re-reading the above has me thinking "take it in", but, on the other
> hand, doing it myself seems a vital first step in lifelong Porsche
> ownership... I like working on my car, but how do you get to the next level
> of the shadetree? I have no friends with more experience or interest than
> me...
> 
If you really want to work on it, I'd say go ahead and start now.  Whenever
you start, it's going to be the same predicament.  The sooner you start,
the sooner the savings from skipping the mechanic will start.  The first
time to do most jobs seems to cost me the same as taking it to the 
mechanic.  It's the second, third, etc. times that my investment pays
off.

> any tips on having an experienced person available to me to get me through
> the difficult moments? I've thought about trying to approach Porsche shops
> as a volunteer part time shop helper, thinking that if I could even see the
> work being done, the benefit would be enourmous... but, after I imagine
> myself as the shop owner I can't really see swinging this deal...
> 
Like I said above, this list contains a lot of expertise.  Use it.  I doubt
the 'shop helper' idea would work.  Your time would probably be better spent
working on your car.  Btw, are you a PCA member.  You can probably find 
someone in your local region that has experience working on their car that
could give you some tips too.  That way if you do get stuck, he/she may
be able to stop by and give things a look...

> should I take it in this time, but in the meantime look for a $2500 1984
