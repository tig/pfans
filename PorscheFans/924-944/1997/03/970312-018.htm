Mike wrote... (about Hoosier RS302 race tires)
>(i) The tires were brand new and I put them on in the parking lot in the 
>morning.

This was a mistake.  I don't think Hoosier gives any documentation with
their tires, but I was lucky enough to be closely in contact with the 
Western US Hoosier rep and the local Hoosier rep.  Both were interested
to see the progress we made with the AWD race car, apparently we were
their first customers with that type of setup.

To make them last, they must be heat cycled.  Put them on the car on a hot,
dry day and drive them around the city, not doing anything cruel to them.
Once warm, bring them home, take them off and let them cool.  That's it.
The way they described it, you've heat cycled them enough when the sticker
goo is completely worn out, which took about 25km for mine of a bit of
agressive driving.

>(ii) Both front tires were corded and flat spotted.  Both my wife and I have 
>lots of AX seat time and know how to threshold brake.  I have since heard that 
>Hoosiers are much easier to lock up because they are so much lighter (they
have 
>only a fiberglass belt package-- no steel).

I would never insult your driving without first seeing you drive :)  Seriously,
the tire pressure is also crucial for the Hoosier to work right.  In the
3290lb Talon, we ran 45psi front and 40psi rear and they lasted the entire
season.  I am using the rear tires from the Talon as my rear tires on the 
Porsche now and we ran two drivers last year on them.  The car took numerous
first, seconds and thirds.  There was a local guy that ran the same tire
pressure as his R1's and he corded the tire in two events!  On the Porsche,
I'm starting at 44psi front and rear.

>(iii) During the 16th run, I was riding as my wife's instructor and we
actually 
>blew one of the front tires out.  Yup, went right through the belt package!  
>Good thing I had my street tires along.

I would never use these as a street/track tire.  The R1 and Yokos are good
for that, but the Hoosier is *not*.
