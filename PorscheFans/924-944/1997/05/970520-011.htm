> I think that this is probably the first case I've ever seen where the DME
> is actually the problem.  In every other situation I've seen, it was either
> a sensor for the DME, bad wiring, or something else.  Just out of
> curiosity, was the car ever started with a system putting out more than,
> say 15V?  It would seem unlikely that swapping chips caused the damage-- it
> is possible to zap a computer while doing the *installation*, but once it's
> in, if it runs, you're usually OK.  Another way to blow up computers is
> with a bad voltage regulator on your alternator.  A high-voltage surge
> would certainly be capable of blowing something as well.
> 
> But I'm still not entirely convinced that your computer is bad-- you stated
> that it still ran when you push-started the car, and it certainly would
> *not* run without the computer.  So don't throw the old one away just yet
> (unless you have to exchange it for the one you just got, of course).
> 
> Perhaps worth considering is that, with the warmer weather, oil tends not
> to be so thick as it is during the winter.  My own car would be nearly
> impossible to start in the morning when I went through this, but worked
> fine once warm and was less troublesome at midday.  In other words, you may
> still have a drag but not notice it until the weather gets a little
> nastier.  You may not be through with this just yet...
> 
> Another possibility is that certain model years may have slightly different
> computers, and the replacement you purchased may be from a later year.
> Porsche may have either eliminated this minimum crank speed thing or
> reduced it.  But I'm not sure how to verify that, making all this is just
> speculation.
> 
> Regardless, I'm happy that you're back on the road.  Please let us know how
> it performs over the next few days-- I certainly hope that this does fix it

> As for the efficiency of the converted system (aside from the
> lubrication issues), by simply switching over to the new refrigerant
> without changing any components, you'd be operating the system along a
> different thermodynamic map than the ideal, most efficient map.
> Apparently the R-134a runs at a higher pressure, so that in order to
> obtain maximum efficiency you'd have to either replace or modify the
> compressor and both heat exchangers (condensor and evaporator) to run
> with the modified thermodynamic cycle (since R-12 and R-134a have
> different P-V profiles, etc.), plus possibly needing a new drier, hoses,
> and other components to withstand the higher pressures.
> 
> However, this might be simplified (and cheapened) for a slightly less
> than perfectly optimal configuration by modifying the compressor to
> produce higher compression (like a stroker kit! :)or replacing it, and
> simply replacing the necessary seals and increasing the airflow over the
> heat exchangers to increase the rate of heat exchange (like additional
> fans).
