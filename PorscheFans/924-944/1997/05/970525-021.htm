> For every one's interest, it is not permitted by FCC law to "jam" or
> intentionally interfere with any other operational use of radio.  As a
> "HAM", one of the many questions on the FCC exam covers just that issue of
> interference.
> 
> Amateur Radio operators have two modes of operation on the RF spectrum.
> The first is primary and operators have full use of those slices of the
> spectrum when operating within the legal limits of the FCC law.  Secondary
> privileges are granted to some frequencies that are shared (mostly UHF) and
> Amateur Radio operators must stand down if found to be interfering with
> other stations with primary privileges.
> 
> In short, it is a violation of Federal law to interfere with any other
> station, including illegal and unauthorized stations, by any operators (HAM
> or otherwise)!  Amateur Radio operators are particularly sensitive to
> retribution by the law as they will get their operating privileges revoked
> where as non licensed offenders get a slap on the wrist (FCC is pretty lame
> unfortunately).  It is a Federal offense to jam radar!
> 
> As for scanners, I tend to agree with the FCC claiming that ALL RF
> frequencies are open to public listening (with the exception of Cellular
> Phone - outlawed by Congress under protest by the FCC).  Unfortunately,
> local laws can, and do violate those privileges in order to protect law
> enforcement efforts.  The question is if your police have the rights to
> demand that in the face of Federal law.  If you are well heeled, you could
> challenge that law in court, or present a class action challenge.  I do not
> know if "posting" makes much difference
> in this case.  I guess you were lucky you got the detector back, and
