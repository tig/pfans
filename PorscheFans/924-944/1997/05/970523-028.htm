>Secondly, the receiver-drier does two things.  One is to remove any
>moisture in the refrigerant so that as the refrigerant reaches this
>evaporator metering point it doesn't freeze (since evaporator temperatures
>can reach below 30) on the expansion device and restrict the refrigerant
>flow.  Secondly, any refrigerant that might reach the compressor as liquid
>is expanded back into a gas in the r-d before the liquid reaches the
>compressor.  Pouring liquid refrigerant into your a/c compressor is like
>pouring water into your cylinders instead of air and fuel.  It does this
>by having the "in" tube into the r-d so that it empties into the r-d lower
>than the pick-up point.  The "outlet" (or to the compressor) is mounted up
>high so it won't suck liquid into the compressor and usually extends to
>the bottom as well with a "metered" hole in the tube at the bottom to
>allow any residual oil accumulation to return to the compressor.  Yes, a
>small amount of refrigerant gets through this hole when no oil is present,
>but like I said it is metered and designed so the refrigerant that gets
>past would expand into a gas before it gets to the compressor.  That's why
>it is important to get the r-d connected right. 


Speaking of misinformation......

The liquid that comes out of the condenser  enters the dryer....
goes down a tube in the center of the dryer....
up around the tube in the main body of the dryer....
through a filter pad that removes particular contaminate....
through the silica drying agent.....
then exits the dryer....

liquid the whole time!

I agree that liquid at the compressor will destroy it, but you are forgetting
that the flash from
liquid to gas occurs at the expansion valve.  After the expansion
valve/evaporator... it's a
straight shot back to the compressor.  No dryer etc.  to "stop liquid from
reaching the compressor"
No liquid gets back because we have converted it to a gas in the cooling
process.
If it were to go from liquid to gas at the dryer.....
you would have a very COLD dryer and a very WARM car. 

That's about what happens when a dryer starts to get restricted.

It is important not to hook up a dryer backwards as often the reverse flow
will dislodge silica from
behind the filter layer in the dryer sending it into the refrigerant stream.
 
Next stop the expansion valve and it will plug it in a heartbeat.  
Funny how one bead of silica fits so nicely into the expansion valve inlet!

Just for info... at the 38 or so psi that is on the suction side, R12 would
have to be colder than about
30F for it to be a liquid.

Gotta neat little chart in the service manual that shows the temp/pressure
relationship.
Neat little chart also available from most A/C supply shops. 

As for moving oil... gaseous R12 moves oil very poorly.  Liquid does a much
better job.
That's why you use 141 to flush a system. It stays liquid much longer at rood
temperature and carries oil out very fast.
It would take hours to push all the oil out with a  gas.
