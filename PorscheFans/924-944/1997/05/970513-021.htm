> The comments about our "speed limit" are essentially correct.    The
> actual verbeage is "Reasonable and Prudent", which allows county
> JPs and MHP officers an awful lot of leeway.    At night the speed limit is
> set at 55 on secondaries, and 65 on the freeways (which are now 
> PAVED, by the way!).   But during the day you CAN do a lot more,
> yet there still seem to be some practical limits.     Almost anywhere,
> 80 seems to be a trigger.    On a wet secondary, above 80 will get 
> you stopped, but not necessarily a ticket.    On a dry, empty,
> straight freeway, driving a capable car with good tires, 100 will
> MOST LIKELY get you stopped, but you may not get a ticket, or you
> may not get convicted.   Sound uncertain?   Well, it is.
> 
> What does this all mean?    Having taken a careful consumer
> survey of most of the central and western Montana highways over
> the two years since the limit was lifted, I can tell you a couple of
> things.   First, speeding at night will get you a deer through the
> windshield.   It is not a matter of if, but when.    Second, most
> secondary highways will NOT support much above 80 anyway,
> because they can be so windy that you just can't see far enough
> ahead to drive faster than that.    Even Wolf Creek Canyon, where
> I-15 passes through a spectacular cut through red mudstone, CAN
> be driven above 100, but one little May snowfall, just a little bit of
> traction sand, and you will buy a concrete wall faster than you can
> say "Aw shit".   Finally, if you DO want to drive that fast, you have to
> be concerned about overtake speed.    While conditions might be
> perfect, blowing by one of our senior citizens with a 90 mph 
> RELATIVE speed doesn't endear you to the friendly villagers
> and can get you turned in via cell phone.    And if you are passing
> on a secondary at such speeds, the guy coming the other way
> can be going even faster.
> 
> It is awfully nice to cruise at 80 without looking over your shoulder
> all the  time.
