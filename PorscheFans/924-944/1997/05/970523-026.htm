>Well, you're still a little off base.  First off the compressor turns a
>low pressure gas into a high pressure gas (not gas into liquid!).  It is
>the condensor coil (the one in front of the radiator) that turns the high
>pressure gas into a liquid.  You are correct though that when that liquid
>reaches the evaporator is again turns into a gas as it "expands".  The
>refrigerant is metered into the evaporator coil and expands (returns to
>gas) as it travels through the evaporator.

Of course!  It makes a whole lot more sense to have the compressor dealing
with only gases since a multi-phase pump is costly and not really
necessary.  Well, one of these days I'll get it straight!   =:-)  Thanks
for the correction.

>Secondly, the receiver-drier does two things.  One is to remove any
>moisture in the refrigerant so that as the refrigerant reaches this
>evaporator metering point it doesn't freeze (since evaporator temperatures
>can reach below 30) on the expansion device and restrict the refrigerant
>flow.

At least I got the first part right, about water I mean.

>Secondly, any refrigerant that might reach the compressor as liquid
>is expanded back into a gas in the r-d before the liquid reaches the
>compressor.

What you're describing here is a sort of metering device and I was under
the impression that the receiver-drier was between the condenser and the
evaporator.  What I read here would be best suited between the evaporator
and the compressor, wouldn't it?

>Pouring liquid refrigerant into your a/c compressor is like
>pouring water into your cylinders instead of air and fuel.  It does this
>by having the "in" tube into the r-d so that it empties into the r-d lower
>than the pick-up point.  The "outlet" (or to the compressor) is mounted up
>high so it won't suck liquid into the compressor and usually extends to
>the bottom as well with a "metered" hole in the tube at the bottom to
>allow any residual oil accumulation to return to the compressor.


Either I've got it all wrong or maybe you're describing something else.
The higher outlet on the receiver-drier makes sense for separating liquids
and gases (liquids are heavier).  But the high-pressure side of this system
only extends to the expansion valve, so the compressor can't really suck
refrigerant from the receiver-drier directly-- it can only pull in what
refrigerant is found in the evaporator, which is essentially low-pressure
(at least compared to the condensor).  So, I'm not sure what you're
describing in terms of the compressor pulling refrigerant from the
receiver-drier.  Could you clarify?
