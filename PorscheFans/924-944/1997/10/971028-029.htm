Hi Maurice and Kevin,
Your arguments sound plausible, but I think there is one fatal error: the
pivot point is not the caliper since that is fixed to the axle and
counteracted by that, it is, I believe in fact, the point of contact of the
tire with the road surface.  Think of it this way: if the caliper were
replaced by a bolt (i.e. the axle locked and unable to turn) if you tried
to push the car the point of resistance (pivot point) would be the contact
of the tire with the road.  It wouldn't matter whether you bolted the
caliper or put a pin through the axle.  It is the same with the rear
wheels. Thus it really makes no difference where you put the calipers, or
even if you use drum brakes, the car dives because the rear is raised up,
transferring weight forward to compress the front springs because the
center of mass is above the tire patch.  By your argument, if you put the
caliper as high as possible and lowered the center of mass of the car below
it, the rear of the car would move down, not up.  This is clearly not going
to happen.  There may be some other reasons for placement of the calipers,
but is should have very little effect on the nose dive.

>Hi Kevin,
>All the reasons mentioned by you make perfect sense. Would you consider 
>the following however. Wouldn't calipers placed behind the frontaxle and 
>in front of the rearaxle reduce the tendany to nose-dive during braking? 
>Upon braking when the car is moving at a given velocity, the mass in 
>motion will resist the change in speed causing a change in the current 
>equilibrium of forces acting upon the mass. As the mass reduces speed a 
>new equilibrium is constantly set, transferring forces in the direction of 
>the motion. At this particular instant, the caliper will be the natural 
>pivotpoint of the mass as this is the place the braking force is exerted. 
>Because the car can only pivot over its axes, it will be harder to pivot
>(read: dive) over the frontaxle if the caliper is behind it. Why? Well 
>because the length of the lever (read: length of the car IN FRONT OF the 
>pivotpoint) is reduced with the difference in cms/inches caused by placing 
>the calipers behind or in front of the axle. 
>The opposite is true for the rearaxle. Bly placing the caliper in front of 
>the axle, the pivotpoint is brought forward, reducing the leverage forces. 
>Physics classes are way back when, so I'm open for corrections.
