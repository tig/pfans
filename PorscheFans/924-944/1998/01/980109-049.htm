Okay, first remember what Price says (in the same paragraph) - "turning
slows the car down" In other words, if all you do is move the wheel, the
car will slow down *more* than if you didn't. Similarly, there are two ways
of loading the front suspension, getting on the brakes, or, getting off the
accelerator.

So, if, like he wants, you're at 100% at the apex, there are many different
ways to get there, and if you can "go with the flow" (of the track), then
why not try it?

>early apexing and early acceleration out of a corner, but it seems to
>contradict all the textbook admonitions to be either hard on the brakes or
>hard on the throttle.

Um, what textbooks? Ever since Piero Taruffi defined the technique of Motor
Racing in, what, 1950?, all road-racing textbooks have included a neutral
phase.

What *has* changed is tire properties and ground-effects (and most of us
don't worry about ground-effects).

One has to be much more controlled and gentle on tires now to (usually) get
the most out of them. The theory hasn't changed, but the slip angles have.

BadBob951 has asked about Centerforce clutches.  No one I know has tried one.
You all may recall that in the past I've posted about an '86 951 I drove which
has a Milledge-built, Motec-equipped 435 bhp motor.  For this application,
Milledge specified some sort of 935 clutch.  Most of the folks around here
with street/track or track only cars that aren't pumped up beyond 350 bhp go
with the Sachs HD unit.  I am not sure how much money the Centerforce clutch
would set you back, or what other changes (if any) are required, but I
wouldn't spend a dime more than the Sachs HD price unless my engine builder
said it was required.

Since I don't have an engine builder, and my cars make only maybe 280 ('89)
and  320 ('87) bhp, I'll stick with the Sachs.
