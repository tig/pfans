>The 928 list totally explored/debated the subject of thread torques vs
>lubricants and anti-seize.  As usual, there are several experts on the
>subject (no pun) in these lists (I'm not one of them - simply reporting
>here).  Application (vibration, heat cycles, materials, safety, etc) may
>dictate thread locker, as well as anti-seize.   The key point is that
>fasteners do not depend on 'friction' to do their job:  it's tension
>between the nut and bolt (against those threads) that provides the
>holding power and where torque is measured.   This whole debate started
>over discussion about use of anti-seize on lug nuts.   It was
>established that torque specs are obtained due to stretch of the bolt
>inside the nut - and not using anti-seize on that application risks
>galling (and having to chisle lugs off the car).   The mechanics are the
>same in all threaded applications:  tension between threads of the two
>elements.

> Hello,
>   Been away from the list for a few days. I picked up on the thread on
> Farzaan's head stud problem and figured I throw in my two cents worth.
> By the way sorry to hear about the problems Farzaan.
>   Correct me if I'm wrong Farzaan but, it sounds like your head studs
> stripped out during the nut torquing and not during a head stud
> installation. If this is the case, what was applied to the block end of
> the thread would have little bearing on the stud pulling out of the
> block. Don't get me wrong here because anti-seize should never be used
> in the installation of either the head studs or the head bolts -- period.
> While some people believe that locktite should be applied to the block
> end of the head studs, Raceware specifically recommends against it.
> What they do recommend is the application of a small amount of pipe
> thread sealant. Application of locktite (or pipe thread sealant) allows
> the cylinder head nuts to be removed without backing the stud out of
> the block. Raceware has found that pipe thread sealant provides enough
> counter torque to allow the cylinder head nuts to be removed without
> the studs backing out which makes the studs much easier to remove down
> the road if needed. Anti-seize applied to the block end of the threads
> would allow the studs to back out when the cylinder head nuts are
> removed.
>    As far as the nut end of the head stud, the only thing that should be
> applied to the threads is a small amount of engine oil.   It is possible
> that if anti-seize was applied to the cylinder head end of the threads,
> an excessive amount of torque could have been applied to the stud causing
> it to be pulled out of the block.
>    I'll throw out some theory on the original equipment head studs here
> for the list to discuss. This is based on reading the section on
> stretch bolt theory on the Raceware home page. According to Raceware,
> stretch bolts (which applies to the 944 head studs) permanently stretch
> and do not return to their original configuration when the torque is
> removed. This is Raceware's basis for recommending that stretch bolt
> should never be reused. While I am somewhat skeptical of this contention
> (in that it would only help Raceware sell more head stud kits) the
> theory is actually quite sound. If this is true, it is possible
> that attempting to torque a previously torqued stretch bolt
> (i.e. 944 head studs) to original specifications might exceed the
> acceptable torque for the aluminum threads in the block. The same
> problem could arise if you attempted to torque non-stretch bolts
> (i.e. Raceware studs) using the factory procedure for stretch bolt
> torquing. Of course, this is only a theory. I also realize that many
> people have reused 944 head studs with no problems (myself included).
> Based on Farzaan's problem, I may consider not reusing 944 head studs
> again.
