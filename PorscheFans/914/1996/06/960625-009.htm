Thought I'd jump in here and add my .02.  I'm a bit of a lurker on this list 
as I have other pcar iron but sometimes get relevant stuff from you dudes.  I 
had a problem with the 912 at the track.  When hot, no start.  Has the hot 
start kit on it.  It had a 'new' rebuilt starter.  I mentioned this to Rich 
Bontempi (Mr.914), of High Performance House and he told me that when they 
rebuilt starters they often didn't replace the solenoids.  I bought a new 
solenoid and voila!  No more hot start problems.  Had the same problem with my 
944.  New rebuilt starter, no hot start.  Same solution.  Haven't followed 
this thread very closely so if you've already trod upon this territory, sorry 
for duplication.  BTW, I'm working on getting Bontempi on line.  He'd be a 
great asset for the list and probably has more 914 parts and knowledge than 
anyone I know.

Jeff has hit on the other major cause to hot/cold starting
problems (as far as cranking is concerned).

  I forgot about this, cuz its been several years since
I lithium'd my solenoid. . .

  Oddly enough, I first caught this problem on an Opel
Kadette station wagon. . .The starter solenoid was dusty dry.
  Lubing it brought it back to life.

 ***   File this one away under hot/cold start problems    ***

  If the Starter wont crank. . .
  Cause #2
  Keep the starter solenoid clean and lubricated with
high and low temperature range grease (I use white lithium).
  If it goes dry its bad, but if the grease turns to wax when its
 -20C, the solenoid will freeze up just the same -with motor
cranking and cranking. . ..  Dont ask how I know. . .

>Hi all,
>
>Here's another spin (sorry) on the hot starter/hot switch problem, but
>it has a happy ending. I've had my 914 for 5 years, with some club track
>events thrown in and have probably had just about all of the (minor)
>failure modes there are.
>
>I had a summer no-start problem that was especially common AFTER a
>really hot run, like the day after a track event. But it wasn't every
>time.  I finally tore into the starter solenoid when it conked out in a
>parking lot (this is Tokyo) and all the Japanese were shaking thier
>heads and muttering "yep. glad I drive a Toyota." (Forgetting that there
>aren't many 20 year old Toyotas even on the road. (Thanks, sometimes
>it's good to just let it all come out.:-))
>
>Anyway, after cleaning the grounds and all connections, checking the
>cable resistance and finding everything OK, I pulled the starter and
>took off the solenoid.  It was OK, but the grease inside the drive unit
>(the part with the little gear on it that jumps out to engage the ring
>gear when the solenoid pushes on it) was almost a powder by now.
>
>To get at it, you can pull it out, if I remember it sort of spirals out
>like a big cork screw. I libeally sprayed in a lot of WD-40 to loosen up
>and desolve the crud and followed it up with some teflon-type dry
>lubricant, some motorcycle chain lube and whatever else was handy and
>looked good for high temperatures. Odd as it may seem, it worked and has
>not been a problem for two summers since. I didn't even change the
>brushes.
>
>My theory is most of this trouble is caused by 20 year-old grease that
>has turned to glue. The solenoid and battery can't overcome the added
>mechanical resistance. Change the grease and it works fine.
>
>Anyone else try this?
>
>914 repair tip # 59346: Clean the contacts and repack the grease.
