> The heater needs to be on for about 1

Actually, there are heat requirements for a couple of reasons - first,
the bare metal and paint should be at fairly uniform temperatures
(depends on what you are spraying) within the span of temp ranges. Two
part epoxy primers, single stage, etc. all have various requirements. 
Second, the three days of heat after spraying is for proper curing, and
again, dependent on what you're priming with.  Also, keep in mind that
humidity is a factor as well, and you should spray on low humidity days
(yes, even in winter).

In any case - air-borne, atomized, flammable paint/primer particles are
*not* (read "DON'T DO IT!") a good thing to mix with any spark or
flame.  If its possible, your heat source should be in a different,
separate, enclosed room with its own source of ventilation. You can duct
the heat into your spray area with a short run of hot air furnace
ducting (and place a furnace filter over its outlet to minimize dust). A
very good quality respirator (one designed for body/paint work) is an
absolute requirement.

Finally, while enclosing a heat source and ducting it might seem like a
royal pain in the backside, the problem is that while you are spraying
and then exhausting the fumes to be able to re-start the heat - the
temperature inside can drop out-of-range very quickly (especially in
colder regions). In fact, the temperature of the metal that you're
spraying will drop faster then the temp of the primer (not a good thing
- especially if you start on one part of the car at 70 degrees and your
last panel is at 40 degrees.).  Additionally, to exhaust the area until
there is absolutely no trace of fumes might take additional hours
depending on the size of the spray area.

In short, I live in a cold region as well and typically wait till there
is a two day forecast of 50+ degree weather to spray. And, BTW, after an
initial 12 hour cure time, most primers become more tolerant to
temperature fluctuation.

Good luck!
