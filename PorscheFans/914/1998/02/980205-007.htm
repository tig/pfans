thought this might be of interest over here...

Caveats:  The context of this particular discussion was racing
motorcycles, specifically 125cc two-stroke GP bikes that spend most of
their lives in the 10K to 13K RPM range.  Of other note is that this
discussion came about on how to recycle sparkplugs for said machine, as
they cost $25.00 each, and electrode wear at those frequencies can cause

> I did my master's work in the field of high repetition rate (20-100
> kHz), high voltage (250 kV) spark gap switches, which are just some
> VERY large, VERY high voltage and current spark plugs. The basic
> principles that govern the breakdown or firing of a spark plug are
> fairly simple, but generally misunderstood, at least as far as I've
> seen from this list (not so much) and the Toyota 4x4 and general
> Toyota I also subscribe to (I gave up on rec.autos.4x4 long ago).
> There is a lot of hoopla on high energy ignition systems that is a
> mix of some truth and some marketing technospeak. I'll try to
> overcome these misconceptions.
> 
> The job of the ignition system is to initiate combustion. To do this
> it generally needs to supply a certain amount of energy into the
> combustible fuel-air mixture in the combustion chamber. There have
> been studies showing the effect of the total energy deposited. Once
> you've initiated a good flamefront, anymore energy is a waste of $$.
> 
> Now take a given engine, so we have a certain compression ratio and
> fuel-air ratio. For a given electrode geometry of the plug, the
> voltage required to fire is a function of the pressure-distance
> product of the gas/distance between the electrodes.  Since the
> pressure is fixed, then the only variable is the distance between the
> electrodes. It's this that determines at what voltage the plug fire.
> It turns out that for the case of interest this is a linear
> dependance. Double the gap and you double the voltage required to
> fire the plug.
> 
> Now, the ignition system MUST be able to generate the appropriate
> voltage. If not, since the pressure in the combustion chamber is a
> function of time (crank position) then the plug can fire at the wrong
> time, ie misfire. Also for a given ignition system the energy
> deposited is ~ related to the length of the arc, so bigger is better.
> This assumes the coil and other components can supply the required
> voltage/energy. 
> 
> So, first off to maximize the current ignition system try opening
> your plug gap. The main problem is that eventually you reach the
> point the gap is too great to breakdown and you misfire. So near max
> gap you can't take much wear to the electrodes before you misfire.
> 
> Now when the plug wears..... The gap changes, but so does the
> geometry as the wear is concentrated near the edges, causing the
> center electrode to round.  It's actually the loss of the sharp edge
> that might be the problem. This sharp edge causes a enhancement in
> the local electric field. This is similar to a mechanical stress
> concentration at a corner. It's actually the local electric field
> stress which causes breakdown. As the center electrode rounds,the max
> field stress for a given applied voltage lessens. Eventually to the
> point of misfire.
> 
> You can file this electrode flat if you want to reuse a plug. To
> increase the energy deposited, increase the breakdown voltage of the
> plug as this increases the amount of energy stored in the coil prior
> to breakdown. It's the total circuit which determines this.
