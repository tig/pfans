I'm using the original '74 901 transmission right now. It's held up for 3,500
miles so far. I'm using a lightweight flywheel manufactored by Patrick
Motorsport's in Phoenix, AZ. Its 6.85 pounds and has the "teeth" notched in the
edge of it for the DME speed sensor. Some minor fitting is required to the
speedsensor so that the gap is correct between the sensor and flywheel.

I'm also using the Patrick Motorsport engine mount bar, so the shift linkage
clears the engine no problem. However, the shift linkage, with the factory bend,
does not clear the 1.75" 914/6 headers. If you're familiar with the construction
of the shift linkage, you'll recall there are two "set-screws"(?) at each end that
hold it in place. The clear the headers the bar simply need to be rotated down
(about 30 degrees) so I drilled another hole in the bar so the set screw now
enters at 30 degrees from original. I can't tell a difference from the factory
set-up.

Other than that, everything is stock except for solid transmission mounts. The
plan is to install a 915 or G50 when the time/money arrives, but 901's are cheap.

Yes, it passed emissions no problem and is my daily driver.

> >INTAKE
> >The intake required significant modification to fit in the 914. The
> >first thing to remove is both of the big boxes on top of the motor: the
> >blower for heater (kwaheri kitabu heat!) and the large box for the air
> >filter. These will not even come close to fitting. My car does not have
> >heat anymore, so that was an easy solution for the blower. Most of the
> >blower can simply be unbolted, but then there is a duct that is used to
> >draw air from the fan. In the picture you can see where I had to cut off
> >this duct and seal it with, of course, signal oranged powder coated
> >sheet metal.
> >There are two of the aluminum brackets on the manifold that support the
> >blower and air box hit the 914's trunk so they must be removed. I opted
> >to remove all the brackets, since none were used anymore, and I think it
> >made the intake manifolds look very nice (plus, I must have saved close
> >to 4 ounces in overall weight!).
> >The air filter was replaced by a smaller cone-shaped element from MSDS.
> >This is also supposed to increase horsepower, but I doubt it. My
> >motivation for using this filter was to make it fit. The next step for
> >air entering the engine after the filter is the air flow sensor. This
> >too is too big for the 914, but you need it to make the DME work. My
> >solution was to cut off the clip on the sensor and solder wires directly
> >to it. I installed a plug on the end of the new wires to plug into the
> >DME harness. This allows that side of the sensor to fit flush against an
> >the manifold. Because my goal was to having nothing sticking out of the
> >engine compartment the air flow sensor had to be flipped over from its
> >normal orientation (so now the plug, where I soldered wires, is facing
> >the fan, instead of the flywheel). Part of it now resides in the trunk,
> >but the trunk seal can be preserved. The mass airflow sensor is
> >connected to a large, rubber boot that finally connects to the intake
> >manifold. This boot has several rubber flanges on it to support vent
> >lines. These flanges are also in the way of the trunk, but if removed
> >(the are only "holders" for the vent lines), the boot fits perfectly
> >(about 4mm clearance).
> >In 1995, I believe, the aluminum intake manifold on 964 engines was
> >replaced with a fiberglass-reinforced plastic manifold. The mass
> >air-flow sensor also was changed to remove the "barn door" style sensor
> >that had been around for decades. If you're considering a 3.6 liter
> >conversion, it may be wise to consider getting this late style induction
> >as well. Not only is there a significant increase in horsepower (and
> >cost), but the components are smaller and lend themselves well to the
> >914 conversion. Maybe that's why they switched? Didn't think so.
> >Anyways, I had to notch my rear trunk to have the inefficent barn-door
> >style sensor fit, and I believe the newer sensor could fit without
> >modification to the rear trunk. Also, the aluminum is not difficult to
> >work with, but it may be easier to remove the brackets that don't fit if
> >it were plastic.
> >The 964's all new, adaptive, Motronic engine management system is very
> >impressive. While it was difficult to get to work the first time, it's
> >been nothing but pleasure since. It maintains idle so well, and is
> >incredibly smooth, it would be difficult to sell me on carbs even if
> >there was a significant increase in horsepower. It performs a bunch of
> >neat tricks, as well, that are fun in a 24 year car; such as
> >automatically retarding the egnition on one cylinder if its knocking, or
> >keeping the idle up 300 RPM's before its warm.
