JJ Slowik wrote:

> This is the same technique that I use, and I find that it comes in handy in
> just everyday driving in bad weather.  When downshifting on slick roads, if
> the friction between the tires and the road isn't enough to bring the drive
> train up to speed when you let out the clutch, the engine dies and then
> you're really screwed.  By bringing the engine up to speed before letting
> the clutch out, you eliminate this risk.  Now, I've always wondered:
> 
> 1.  Why is *this* technique called double-clutching when you're not actually
> pressing the clutch twice?

Good point. It shouldn't be called 'Double clutching'. It's heel-toeing.

> 2.  If you do it Dave's way (which *is* double clutching), what benefit to
> you gain?  I suppose that if your transmission didn't have syncros at all,
> then double-clutching would almost be necessary, but in a synchronized
> transmission, it strikes me as a little pointless.  Now, I'm by no means an
> authority on this (or any...) subject, so don't take offence, just help me
> understand.

The reason why I double clutch every down shift is to _save_ my syncros.
914s are notoriously abusive on thier soft syncro rings, and I'd like to
do everything I can to preserve the life of my transmission. I'd rather
sink $750-1k into custom 3pc racing wheels than into rebuilding my
transmission. 1st and 2nd gear syncors if not babied will generally last
about 30k miles tops. Yes, replacing just the syncro and dog-teeth isn't
too terribly expensive, but I'm always falling victim to
"while-I'm-in-there-itis".
