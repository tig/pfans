>4. What's the best way to straiten the car before welding?
>
  The 4 donuts on the underside of the floorpan are the measuring
reference
points used by the factory and aftermarket body shops for checking the
overall
body for dimensions. . .these are in book 8 of the factory manuals, but
you may
be able to get the dim's from a body shop that does VW's and other
foreign
car body work.  This is computerized nowadays.
  Performing the actual 'straightening' is best done at a body shop
using
a 'blackhawk' or at least a concrete floor with drag points built in for
chains and come-alongs, but this is highly dependent on the the extent
of
the twist or warp in your body.  *YMMV*  heh, heh.  I have seen people
use
a bumper jack and chains/come-alongs and jackstands, cinder blocks
and trees or utility poles, bridges, etc.  :)

>5. I was thinking of temporarily supporting the front and the back with
>beams welded half way between the       center tunnel and the longs and
>then do the longs.
>
  I did the 'beams' method once, and now my doors dont fit. . .now I
need
to do the 'chains and come-alongs' gig to correct my body's warp and
twist. . .
  Your best bet is to 'jig' the doors.  By that I mean remove the doors
(you
have to anyway) and install a frame that bolts to the door opening at
the
hinges and the latch point.  I would take it one further by tack welding
the
jig frame up high on the sail panel and down low at the corner of the
'A'
pillar.  Remove and refinish after you complete the rocker/longitudinal
repairs.  Factory book 8 describes this jig. .maybe even a pix.  The
problem
with 'beaming' is that there is tremendous force involved when a lot
of linear welds (such as doing a longitudinal) cool.  I think having the
door jig spreads the force over the door opening, preventing the weld
shrinkage from accumulating.
