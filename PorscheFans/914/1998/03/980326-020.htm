Josh Hadler wrote:

> Hey all,
>
>         Well, the O2 sensor has been installed, tomorow I'll mount the gauge
> and see how it works. For those interested, here's what I can tell you
> so far.
>
>         The sensor is mounted into the Bursch collector just upstream of the
> muffler in the 180 degree bend of the last pipe. This allows easy access
> while still keeping it out of the way of debris or other such stuff. It
> is a three wire variety (+12, GRND, and signal). The ground wire I have
> mounted to the rear ground at the tranny (shorter wire, easier to
> install), the hot wire and signal wire I have running into the passenger
> compartment (Dave, remeber those extra wires we installed? :). So the
> hot wire will go to switched power, which is just fine, and probably be
> used to bring power to the display as well. And the signal, well that's
> obvious.
>         The display is a single digit resolution LCD display which seems to be
> about 1.5x3 inches in size, nice and big. It reads out raw voltage from
> the sensor. O2 sensors have an output of 0-1V, 0.5 being stoichiometric
> (14.7). The designer of this system has told me that the best settings
> will be around 0.4 or 0.5 for freeway cruising, and closer to 0.7 for
> racing. He felt that the big single digit would be the best as A) that's
> really all the resolution any sensor can provide, and B) that's all one
> is going to remeber in the 'heat of battle' anyway. The 3-wire sensor is
> faster, and more accurate, but does not have a very wide dynamic range.
> So if the engine is either very lean or very rich, it won't yield a
> reliable signal. That's ok though anyway, if it's that bad, there'll be
> other symptoms as well I'm sure. This also means that the 0-1V range for
> the 3 wire will yield a higher resoltion that a two wire will.
>
>         It seems that all said and done, it took about 2-3 hours to install,
> and the hardware itself will cost about $140.
