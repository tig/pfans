> The mixture gets leaned out why? I'm guessing it's to prevent
> detonation, but I could easilly be wrong.

    Yeah, I think you're wrong... Detonation is usually handled by
    RAISING the fuel/air ratio, not lowering it.

> What exactly is the mechanism of a knock sensor, and what
> physically is it detecting. In other words, what exactly is making
> the sound/shock/vibration that the KS is picking up?

    Knock sensors are pretty tricky... They detect detonation either
    by directly measuring the gas pressure in the cylinders or by
    "listening" to the vibration on the outer surface of the
    cylinders.

    Either way, it's tough to distinguish between detonation and
    normal running; physical placement of the sensors is critical, a
    fair amount of filtering and thresholding of the signal is
    required, and knock sensors are individually "tuned" to the
    particular engine models they're installed on.

> On another note, the mixture obviously goes _way_ lean when the
> throttle is fully closed above idle.
> ....
> doesn't it seem to be a little unhealthy for the engine to be
> running so lean at higher revs (albiet for short periods of time)?

    No, it's not unhealthy... There's no load on the engine when the
    throttle's closed, so the lean running doesn't hurt anything.

    The computer DELIBERATELY shuts off the fuel flow when the
    throttle's closed, then starts the flow again when the revs drop
    to near idle-speed (or when you reopen the throttle).  Shutting
    off the fuel on the overrun has two advantages:

        1.  It increases gas mileage.
        2.  It GREATLY improves throttle response when you open the 
            throttle again.

    Unfortunately, cutting the fuel on the overrun also keeps the
    car from making those great crackling backfires and belching
    flame out its exhaust whenever you close the throttle.

    Oh, well... Can't have everything, I guess.
