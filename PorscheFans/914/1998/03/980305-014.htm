> To the List,
> Exhaust studs:
> 
> Lot of discussion on these. The exhaust studs have been in most 914's for
> over 22 years. Heat is what makes these suckers feel like there are
> extreme
> critical dimensions on the threads. The studs are normal 8mm threads and
> the
> mating holes the same standard.
>  
> Non-similair metals under well heated conditions, such as the exhaust
> system
> are subject to, will corrode no matter the thread tolerances and size.
> Moisture is also the culprit combined with the prolonged heating and
> cooling
> cycles.
> 
> When you install studs, always use an anti-seize. The factory did, but in
> the time frame mentioned above, the heat will eventually "cook" the
> anti-seize into a useless state and contribute to the corrosion seen on
> the
> studs as seen when removing the heat exchangers and complete exhaust
> system.
> 
> There is a saying. "Don't fix it if it ain't broke". True, but remove the
> exhaust system nuts periodially and coat the 8 stud threads and the
> retaining nuts with a good anti-seize and save yourself possible internal
> thread damage later in life. This should be a twice yearly maintenance
> procedure on newly installed studs. You don't physically have to remove
> the
> exhaust system, just all 8 of the 8mm self-securing nuts, one at a time.
> Never tighten more than 23.1 ft.lb. if torqueing. This torque is the head
> torque figure for my 70 1.7. Correct this figure to the list if you
> disagree.
> 
> For the ones that have corrosion. 
> Spray them good with a good rust penetrating product. Use the small
> plastic
> tube that normally will be found on the can and squirt up INTO the nuts.
> Let
> sit 30 minutes then do it again. Let it work overnight if possible. 
> Before removing nut, spray again and back nut off about 1  complete
> revolution. Do each like this. Once you can get the nut down some, spray
> the
> top side of the nut and let it sit for 15 minutes. Then proceed to remove
> nuts as above.  However, if the studs begin to turn when backing off the
> nut, spray up above the nut into the threaded hole as soon as there is
> room
> to do so. This will help to keep the threads intact as much as possible,
> but
> is no guarantee that they will not pull material when screwing out the
> stud. 
> ALWAYS WEAR EYE protection when working under the 914. 
> 
> Get a small acid brush from your auto supply store and use it for coating
> the studs and nuts with the anti seize. After installing the nuts, coat
> the
> lower portion of the studs again to restrict future moisture and
> corrosion.
> 
> Naturally, this is my own opinion and yours may be different. What works
> for
> me may not work for you. Use your own discretion as you alone are
> responsible for what you do to meet your own needs.
