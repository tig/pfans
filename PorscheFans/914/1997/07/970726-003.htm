You should not need to use more than maybe 1.5L of brake fluid for a
complete and thorough flushing.

>   Second, the things that don't work.   Vacuum bleeders, i.e.
> mighty-vac, etc.  These merely invert the seals on the calipers, and
> suck air into the caliper past the piston.    Rule one: NO VACUUM!

Even mopre simply than that, they draw air in around the bleed nipple.
It's very hard to get a very good seal on the nipple for these things to
work well. As a result, it's nearly impossible to tell if teh air you're
drawing in is coming from the brake system or not. But yes, vacuum
bleeders, in my experience, are unreliable.

>   Rule two:  No pressure bleeders.  The air collects in the master
> cylinder.  Ideally, the master cylinder should be pre-bled on the
> bench before installation.   If not, you will be able to get most of
> the air out of it, but there is only one way to do it.   That way is
> NOT pressure bleeding.   Any attempt to force fluid through the
> system from the reservoir causes the fluid to percolate through the
> master cylinder without sweeping the air out of it.   I got beautiful
> trails of bubbles that finally subsided into clear fluid, and still
> no pedal.   So, repeating rule two:  NO PRESSURE BLEEDERS!

I have had mixed results with pressure bleeders. My conclusion was that
they too were unreliable.

>   Rule three:   Pump The Pedal !!   This is the method given to me by
> Josh, and it is the ONLY thing that  worked at all.   Briefly, the
> technique is this:  It takes two people; one to pump the pedal, and
> the other to manipulate the bleed screws on the calipers.  Be sure
> the brake fluid reservoir is full and kept full, or else you will
> have to start all over.  Have the person in the car press down on the
> pedal.   Go to the caliper farthest from the master cylinder.  Open
> the bleed screw.  Close the bleed screw.   Have the person in the car
> lift their foot, and then press down again.  Open the bleed screw
> only with pressure in the system.  If possible, try to close it on
> the last part of the down stroke of the pedal when fluid is still
> moving.   Repeat until all bubbles dissapear.  Use a 1 quart catch
> jar.  If you have not moved a quart of fluid through each caliper,
> you have not caught all the air yet. Keep going.   Bleed both bleed
> screws if your calipers have two.  Notice I said a QUART of fluid per
> caliper.  This may be a slight exageration, but ONLY slight.  I would
> lay in AT LEAST ONE GALLON of brake fluid before starting to bleed.
> Chances are, you will need more than that.

Ok, lets define some terms here. At least how _I_ define them. 

Bleeding: extracting air and water contaminated fluid that resides NEAR
the calipers. 
Flushing: complete exchange of old fluid for new fluid in the entire
system. 

When bleeing OR flushing, definately start at the furthest caliper
first. Right Rear, then Left Rear, then Right Front, and then Left
Front.

My technique for flushing may be a bit weird, but it has proven
extreemly effective. I buy two types of fluid, one is Motul 600 degree
racing fluid (yellow), and the other is Ate super blue. Both are very
high temp fluids, and require frequent exchanges because of the
hydophilic nature of high temp brake fluids. Anyway, I alternate the two
brands and because one is yellow and the other blue, I can tell when the
line has been thoroughly flushed. It goes from yellow, to green, to
blue, and presto! No excess fluid used on guesswork. And as a result I
use significantly less than a _gallon_ of brake fluid. And this is
expensive brake fluid here, so I'm inclined to save as much of my money
as I can. Also, draw out as much of the old fluid from the reseviour as
you can and replace it with new stuff, this will mean less time bleeding
to get the new fluid down to the master.
