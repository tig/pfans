Dirk Wright wrote:

> If the center of mass is further back in the car (toward the 
> rear wheels), the distance that the center point has to traverse 
> is less, thus causing less of an upset in the car's attitude from 
> sticking to sliding. Cars with lowest polar moment of interia 
> (determined by the distance from the center of mass to the center 
> of the rear wheels) are the most stable in transitioning from sticking
> to sliding.

My understanding of polar moment of interia is different and I
am confused by the description given above.  My definition is 
the resistance of the car to forces (sliding, spinnng) 
perpendicular to the direction the car is travelling.

It also has nothing to do with weight distribution and alot to
do with where the weight is located.  A 914 with a near 50/50
distribution and a 928 with a near 50/50 distribution behave 
completely differently.

Since the 914s weight is concentrated at the center of the car
it is much easier for the car to enter into and more difficult
to recover from a spin - it's basically like a top.  Whereas in
the 928 with the engine up front and the transaxle in the rear, 
the weight is distributed more evenly and centered over the wheels.
That makes the 928 extremely forgiving and resistant to spinning.

Regardless of how you corner-balance your 914 you can't change
its polar moment of inertia significantly...
