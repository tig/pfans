  Lastly, this is the advice that I got from Chris Kolstadt; although
I have not tried it, I intend to, because my car still has lean hunt
at idle, and the pot on the brain has no effect; also my idle speed
screw has limited effect, indicating a way-too-lean mixture.   Since
I have already tried a fixed resistor in series withthe Temp Sensor
II with some effect, but still indications of too lean, I am back to
suspecting my manifold pressure sensor.   Here is Chris's advice:

"    I got mine to pass with flying colors.  But there is a
process.  It is "easy" to get a tuned car to pass on the idle
pollutants by just
adjusting your idle adjustment screw on the "brain".  As for the high
rpm
test, that's a little more difficult.  You have to pull out the
pressure
sensor and remove the cover on the end.  It is put in with epoxy, so
you will either have to take a flame thrower to it or use a Dremel
tool
like I did (are't Dremels great?).  The screw that you expose is the
adjustment screw for your over-all mixture.  Pay the guy at the smog
check $10
and tell him you want to adjust your mixture.  Turn your idle mixture
all the
way rich (clockwise I believe which seems backwards) on the brain. 
Then
run the car at the 3000 rpm (or whatever the high rpm limit is) and
adjust
your newly exposed adjustment screw.  It is a little touchy, so be
patient. After your high rpm emissions are passing (try to make them
pass
easily so you don't have trouble when you do the real test), go back
to idle
and adjust the screw on the brain.  Then, just for grins, recheck
your
high rpm limit one more time.
        After doing all this, you should be ready to pass smog. 
We'll keep our fingers crossed for you.

Chris Kolstad
'73 914 2.0 CSP/B    "

   It is my opinion that the "generic" factory adjustment on the MPS
