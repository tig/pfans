Dirk Wright wrote:
> 
> Also, the book has convinced me to convert to hydraulic lifters, though I
> don't know when I'll do that. I can't decide to wait until what I have
> wears out, or tear the case apart and "do it now." I know Dave will say,
> "just drive the damn car!"
> 
> The author states that the hydraulic lifters are good to 6,000rpm, which is
> more than the rest of the valve train can tolerate anyway.

Not true! A -stock- 914 engine, and by that I mean a balanced and
blueprinted but still stock valvetrain, piston, crank etc, can spin up
to 7,000rpm with no damage. There won't be any power up there unless you
do something to the intake, but to say that the 914 engine can't spin up
to even 6,000rpm is wrong.

> What convinced
> me though is that with hydraulic lifters, the valve clearance is
> automatically compensated for temperature changes. Thus the valves always
> have the correct adjustment from cold startup to 90mph blasts down the back
> straight.

So do you intend on competing with this car? I thought you just wanted
to build a cool daily driver Drik? If you really are gonna track it,
read the rules. In some classes a mod like that might mean a big class
jump. Silly I know, but dems da rules sometimes.

> Also, always having the correct adjustment means that there is
> more power available over time and temperature changes. Mechanical lifters
> set the correct clearance at only one temperature. With such a hot running
> motor as ours, hydraulic lifters make a lot of sense to me.

That's why the adjustment is done when cold. It's taking into account
that the engine (and everything else) expands when hot and that the
clearances we use when cold will go away when it heats up.

Yes, hydraulic lifters are a great invention, and allow alot more
versatility. But I don't know the conversion for our motors too well,
and I'm wondering if it can handle higher rpm or not. I'd rather have a
lifter that's fixed and may not be ideal when cold than a lifter that
lags at high rpm.
