Joshua Writes:

> PMI (Polar Moment of Inertia) is the inertia of an object as it relates
> to its rotation (i.e. a car spinning). The mass of the bumper has just
> as much weight (mathematically speaking) in calculating the PMI as the
> mass of the motor. You need to take into account _all_ mass of the car,
> not just that between the front and rear wheels. Hell, there are alot of
> high performance cars, 911s included, that place the engine outside the
> wheelbase while the CM remains inside the wheelbase. If we were to
> exclude all mass that was outside the wheelbase when the center of mass
> is between the wheels in our calculations, we'd be making a grevious
> error. And scaring the piss out of our poor test driver the first time
> he/she took the car out on the test track. =8-O
> 
> My example above yields two very different PMIs, but the CM (Center of
> Mass) remains the same, in the middle. Here's one way of looking at it.
> What is easier to get spinning, a bicycle wheel with a very heavy solid
> rubber tire? Or the same bicycle wheel with the same amount of rubber
> concentrated about the hub instead? Both wheels have the same center of
> mass, but the later example is going to be alot easier to initiate
> spinning and equally so to halt its spinning. Think of it as the rubber
> wheel mass having a much larger lever arm to exert on _your_ force, be
> it acceleration or braking, than the equally massive rubber hub. This is
> why we are much more interested in shaving off every little bit weight
> from the wheels, while weight off the hubs has noticably less
> consequence where it comes to how rapidly we can get the car to
> accelerate. So, get the engine (the singly most massive part of the car)
> as close to the hub (or center of the car) if we want a car that can
> change direction as rapidly as possible. But we have to be prepared for
> the consequences of making a "wheel" able to change states so easilly,
> it will also tend to be much more difficult to coax back into a straight
> line if it gets away from us. We've all been there at one point or
> another, where our 914 has gotten away from us, havn't we? :)
