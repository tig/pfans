	Good description, but I've got a couple of things to add:

At 1:44 AM -0700 6/11/97, Brian Ng wrote:
>_rear brakes_
>Very similar to front brakes, but use the 4mm allen key to retract the
>pistons.

	One IMPORTANT difference--use pliers to remove the "spring clips"
before you remove the pad retaining pins.  Each clip goes through a hole
in its associated pin.  I found out that, if you hit the pin REAL hard
with a punch, you can break the clip and push the pin out that way.  Did
we mention that new clips are kinda hard to find?
	Also, I don't think anybody makes rear pads with the right-sized
holes in them.  You will have to use a drill or reamer (I think it's a
5/16" or 3/8") to open up the top hole so the pin will slide through it.

>The inner piston requires a long 4mm allen key, or my favorite, a 4mm allen
>key 1/4" dr socket that you can put an extension on.  First remove the 4mm
>"cover plug" and then retract the piston in the same manner as the outer
>one.

	Aftermarket inner plugs are not uncommon.  Either look into the
hole through the trailing arm with a mirror, or poke your finger in there
to try to figure out what kind of socket/wrench you need.  My 2.0 has the
AutoMashin' (I think) plugs, with 10mm bolts epoxied onto them.  It was
several days of frustration before I figured that out...

>You don't really have to bleed your brakes, but if the m/c fluid isn't
>clean (ie looks black-ish) flushing the system at this time is a good idea.
> I do a brake flush once a year--fluid is cheep, rebuilding calipers is a
>hassle.

	If you don't flush your brakes, eventually the insides of the parts
will rust from water in the fluid.  If the rust is bad enough, you get to
throw out your old calipers (etc.) and buy some nice shiny new ones.  (Did I
ever mention how much I hate the previous owners of my now-sold 1.8?)
