What class do intend to compete in? Just about any porsche, no matter

I don't get it. What's the difference at that point? You've replaced the

> On another subject, my car is currently being fitted with steel GT 
> flares,7&8 x 15 Fuchs, front sway bar, 320 brake calipers and 
> possibly the 2.0L F.I. motor. Am I going to have to run in modified 
> SCCA classes? I've never competed in SCCA before and I'm not 
> familiar with their rules.

If you're going to run with SCCA, you're going to be running against teh
"trailer-riding-fire-breathing" Prepared class cars. The brake calipers
and modified hubs are not allowed in the lower classes. You can get away
with the five lug wheels in Street Prepared -IF- you use a 4 to 5 lug
adapter plate. But you can't modify the hubs for five lug wheels. It's a
stupid rule, but it's a rule nonetheless.

> If so, how fast and competitive are these guys?

VERY! You would run in either C-Street Prepared (sans calipers) or
A-prepared. I can tell you that both of these classes are very fast. CSP
is mostly a CRX/MR2/RX-7 dominated class, but the miatas are starting to
show up in SP and they are blindingly quick. Oh yeah, and the occasional
M3 too. And AP? It's the Louts class. Take your pick, Elan, 7 or Europa,
thy're all hellishly quick cars. Ask Mike Nugent, in his region, he has
to compete against a Lotus Elan that has helped its drivers to 13
national trophies over its history. He can certainly tell you how fast
that car can be. In local competition, if you don't live in Seattle, the
914 might still do well in AP, as AP is an undersubscribed class
compared to CSP (one of the largest).

> I've heard it's a tough league to play in.

It can be. But I feel that tough competition breeds better drivers. And
the competition and drive makes it all the more fun. And the competition
always gets left on the track. The comraderie in SCCA is great, and the
variety of cars is always fun.

My advice on the motor, get a stock 2.0. It'll save you headaches _and_
money.
