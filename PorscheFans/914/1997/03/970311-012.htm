o.k. now that's out of the way. I would say that the location you
mentioned is probably the simplest, but also the least effective. There
is not going to be much cold air hitting the cooler in that location.
There is heat exhaust from the RHS engine air, radiant heat from the
exchangers, and heat from the tranny as well. The best location is of
course in front of the car. Bumper mounted is usually a very popular
method. I have seen some mounted into the bumper and holes cut in the
front trunk to exhaust the heated air. Although I like the idea of
exhausting the heated air thru the fresh air intake grille and up over
the windshield. That's what alot of the modified cars do. Another
location I've seen is protuding up from the rear trunk. I have seen them
recesed and flush in a rear wing or spoiler. These all require alot of
extra oil and expensive oil lines. It all depends on your budget for
this project. An easier location is of course hanging from the engine
lid. This is the route I took. Partly because of the ease of
installation, and partly because the rules in my class dont allow
cutting of sheet metal.

On any large oil cooler you should already have upgraded the oil system
as much as bolt-in changes can be done. By this I mean higher pressure
relief pistons and a high volume oil pump. Try these first and see how
much the temp comes down. It will, but not by a huge amount. I have a
25-row fan-forced oil cooler mounted to the engine grille, and I can get
up to a 30-40 degree drop in temps from what I was running before. The
fan alone is worth 25 degrees. My whole package cost me about $700
including thermostat and aeroquip lines.

If I were you, look into mounting up front, your engine will thank you.
If it were for a street car, I'd be nervous mounting a big oil cooler up
front. Especiall if the car is lowered. One big piece of road debris,
and that's it.

> One suggestion for oil starvation is an Accusump.  I've heard of them, but
> know nothing about them.  Cost?  Availability?  Do they solve the problem?
>  Is it possible to "drain" the Accusump resevoir?  Are there other solutions
> short of the dreaded dry-sump?

The first step is usually enough. And that is the installation of
extended oil sump, or oil-trap. I have found that I no longer get oil
starvation in sweepers. And it's ALOT cheaper than an accusump, lighter
too. They're about $50 or so. It replaces the plate cover for the oil
strainer with a cup the extends down about two inches or so, and extends
the pickup tube to draw from the bottom of this cup. Works great.
