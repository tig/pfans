> At 10:25 AM 4/22/97 -0500, you wrote:
> >I have been incrementally 'improving' the suspension on my '74 914, and
> >would like a bit 'o advice.
> >
> >Currently the car has:
> >
> >Bilstein shock/struts all the way around
> >Weltmeister 19mm adjustable front anti-roll bar
> >140 lb springs rear
> >Stock torsion bars
> >
> >The car has been lowered on the stock torsion bars to match the 140lb
> >springs, but now has a tendency to oversteer. I suspect that the proper
> >approach would be stiffer torsion bars, but barring that for now, would it
> >be reasonable to set the anti-roll bar to a stiffer setting to help control
> >the oversteer. What handling quirks might be introduced by such an
> >approach?
> >
> >Thanks
> >
> >
> >
> You can do a few things with what you have to solve your oversteer problem.
> 
> 1. Change the rake of the car
> 2. Adjust tire pressures
> 3. Adjust your front swaybar

The best three suggestion for suspension adjustments. But I'd try
reversing those three though. Rake change will effect the front end
alignment and that gets trickier.

> All are free! I'd probably just adjust the front bar assuming the rake of
> the car is right. Remember if you've lowered the car quite a bit you may
> want to shorten the bump rubber on your front struts so you can maintain
> some shock stroke

The problem is oversteer. If the front bumps stops are too large, he
would be losing travell and bottoming out the front suspension. This
_is_ a problem when you lower a car, but the problem this causes is
_understeer_ not oversteer. The bottoming out can also be caused by worn
out torsion bars. But the problem here is oversteer, so if bottoming out
is a problem anywhere, it'd be in the rear. How long are the rear
springs?
