Michael was kind enough to forward the following to me:

> (From the installation instructions)
> "The HI-6 rev limiter utilizes an automatic sequence selection
> program that learns the optimum firing sequence for your engine,
> based on your engine's response. . . .  When the engine reaches the
> rev limit, . . . only 1 pulse is fired for every 7 trigger signals.
> . . . If engine RPM continues to increase and exceeds 103% of the
> set rev limit, the HI-6 will decrease the firing to 1 of 9.  The
> HI-6 will continue to decrease firing sequence in odd steps (1 of
> 11, 1 of 13, 1 of 15, etc.) until engine RPM drops back below 103%
> of the rev limit.  Within a few revolutions, engine RPM will
> stabilize between 100% and 103% of the set rev limit.  Whenever
> engine RPM drops below 100% of the rev limit, the HI-6 will increase
> the firing sequence stored in memory."

    Mike:

    I like it... It sounds as though Chris Schroeder (Crane Cams'
    software guy) did a good job on that one.

> In autocrossing, it sounds like the rev limiter can be used when
> you hit redline a short space before a corner - not enough time for
> an upshift - you could safely keep the accelerator floored until
> you actually need to back off for the corner, right?

    Yeah, more or less... The only problem is that even with this
    "soft" limit, there are still some rather abrupt transitions
    between the on- and off-limit states.  You'd have to try it and
    see whether the car gets unbalanced as the engine comes on and
    off the limiter.

    One way to do an even SOFTER limit is to progressively retard
    the timing as you approach the limit... That may make for a
    smoother transition.

> I most like the adjustable rev limit (two rotary switches that can
> be set from 600 to 9900 RPM in 100 RPM increments without use of any
> extra cost "pills" like MSD uses) which claims an accuracy of +/-30
> RPM.

    The method I use in my software is better (in my opinion)... You
    rev the engine to exactly one-half of your desired rev limit,
    then hit a button to set the rev-limit to twice the current
    engine speed.  In the last ignition controller I did (which
    actually had TWO rev-limits -- for drag racers) this was
    accurate to +/- 1 RPM at 10,000 RPM.
