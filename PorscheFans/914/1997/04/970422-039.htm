On 4/22/97 5:39 PM, Joshua Hadler wrote:

>Chip Stratton wrote:
>> 
>> I think I may have learned something about sway bars. Tell me if I'm
>> wrong, but for a given amount of anti-roll force, a thicker bar will
>> allow more wheel travel since the pickup point would be farther out on
>> the sway bar arm?
>
>Hmm, it's more that the lever arm to the bar is shorter and hence
>requires a greater torque to move the same amount. As a result, it takes
>a larger weight transfer to achieve the same angle of body roll. What a
>stiffer bar will do is transmit more motion from one side to the other.
>This has the effect of reducing the independance of and independant
>suspension. There are always pros and cons to stiffer sway bars. A stiff
>sway bar will cause the car to skitter over bumpy terain more than a
>softer one will. The nice thing about sway bars though is that you still
>retain a more compliant suspension as far as vertical travel goes. It's
>really a fine balancing act, tuning the spring rate vs. sway rate ratio.
>

Actually if you make the lever arm too short it really does limit the 
suspension travel.  To convince yourself of this, imagine that the lever 
arm were 1" long.  The maximum suspension travel (of the drop link, not 
necessarily the tire) would be 2".  To be any longer, the lever arm 
length would have to be increased.  I have run with a 22mm anti-roll set 
to a lever arm length of < 4" and found that I have lots of little 
problems (like binding in the drop links, and difficult adjusting out 
pre-load) because of the reduction in suspension travel.  I'm now using 
much heavier front springs (once I reached the max torsion bar size), and 
can leave the anti-roll bar set to a more reasonable 5-6" range.

Anyway, it's good to hear that Chip has reduced his oversteer by 
tightening the front sway bar.
